# Motivational interviewing protocols for goal elicitation in populations

with ambivalence or executive dysfunction. Step-by-step clinical technique,
not overview. Evidence base for specific components: open questions,
reflective listening, decisional balancing. Adaptations for populations
with working memory deficits or executive dysfunction. Practitioner manuals
and peer-reviewed studies.

Motivational interviewing (MI) can be operationalized into very concrete micro‑steps for eliciting goals even when ambivalence, working memory limitations, or broader executive dysfunction are present.[^1_1][^1_2]

Below is a **technique-focused** protocol rather than an overview, followed by evidence for key components and specific adaptations for cognitive impairment.

***

## Step‑by‑step goal elicitation protocol (MI micro‑structure)

This assumes you have 20–40 minutes and want to leave with 1–2 collaboratively defined, personally meaningful goals, not a full treatment plan.[^1_2][^1_1]

### 1. Engage and set a narrow focus

Purpose: identify “what to work on today” without demanding long-range planning.

1. Begin with a broad, low‑load open question to invite agenda items:
    - “What would you like to make sure we take care of today?”
    - “What else?” (repeat 2–3 times, then stop).[^1_3]
2. Reflect and **name** possible targets in plain language, keeping responses short:
    - “So, sleep is hard, work is stressful, and you’re not sure where to start.”[^1_1][^1_2]
3. Ask for a *single* focus for this conversation:
    - “Of the things you mentioned, which would you most like us to work on today?”[^1_3]
4. Offer a brief menu if needed (supports initiation):
    - “We could talk about getting through the workday, your sleep, or your energy. Which feels most important right now?”[^1_4][^1_1]

Key process rule: after each open question, offer 2–3 reflections before you ask another question (aim for a reflections:questions ratio >1).[^1_2]

***

### 2. Clarify current state and values (develop discrepancy)

Purpose: connect the chosen area to personally valued roles or directions in life to create “pull” toward change.

1. Elicit a concise description of the current pattern:
    - “Tell me about a recent day at work that shows how this is going right now.”[^1_1][^1_2]
    - Reflect concrete elements, not abstractions: “You’re missing deadlines because starting tasks is hard, even when you know what to do.”[^1_1]
2. Elicit values/identity in simple, concrete terms:
    - “What kind of [worker/parent/student] do you want to be?”
    - “What matters most to you about how your day goes?”[^1_5][^1_1]
3. Use **selective** double‑sided reflections to highlight discrepancy:
    - “On one hand, you feel stuck and drained at work; and on the other, it’s important for you to be someone who follows through and can rely on themselves.”[^1_2]
4. Avoid full, neutral “pros/cons lists” at this stage with highly ambivalent clients; emphasize change talk over sustain talk (see decisional balance evidence section).[^1_6]

***

### 3. Elicit change talk directed toward *goals*, not just symptom reduction

Purpose: evoke the person’s own reasons for a different future state.

Use the DARN‑CAT heuristic (Desire, Ability, Reasons, Need → Commitment, Activation, Taking steps).[^1_7][^1_1]

1. Desire/Need questions:
    - “If things were a bit better with [chosen area], what would be different in your day?”
    - “What do you most want to be different about this?”[^1_1]
2. Reason questions tied to values:
    - “How would changing this help you be more like the kind of worker you want to be?”[^1_5][^1_1]
3. Ability questions, carefully framed to avoid overwhelming:
    - “What’s one thing you’ve done before that shows you *can* move in this direction, even a little?”[^1_1]
4. Reflect and **amplify** change talk; soften or briefly mirror sustain talk:
    - Client: “I know I need to get on top of my tasks, but my brain just shuts down.”
    - Clinician: “You really *need* to get on top of things, and your brain shuts down when you try to start.” (change talk second).[^1_2]

Do not ask for “goals” yet; stay with experiential language (“different,” “better,” “more like you want”).

***

### 4. Move from change talk to provisional goals

Purpose: translate intrinsic motivations into 1–2 concrete, behaviorally anchored goals that are very small and time-bound.

1. Use a *bridging* summary:
    - “Let me check I’ve got this. Being reliable at work matters a lot to you; right now you’re overwhelmed and stuck starting tasks; if things changed, you’d be finishing more and leaving work with less guilt.”[^1_2][^1_1]
2. Ask a **narrow** future‑focused open question:
    - “Given all that, what’s one small change you’d like to see in how your workday goes over the next week?”[^1_8][^1_9]
3. If the person struggles, offer a **menu of micro‑goals** grounded in their narrative:
    - “Some people in your shoes work on: starting one task early in the day, checking a to‑do list twice, or asking for help once. Any of those fit, or is there something else?”[^1_4][^1_1]
4. Once an option is voiced, immediately make it specific and observable:
    - “If we turned ‘starting one task early’ into something you could check off, what exactly would that look like tomorrow?”

Keep working memory demands low by holding to one goal at a time and repeating it aloud.

***

### 5. Sharpen goals using MI‑consistent scaling and planning

Purpose: shape goals for feasibility without slipping into a directive problem‑solving mode.

1. Confidence scaling:
    - “On a scale from 0 to 10, where 0 is ‘no way’ and 10 is ‘absolutely sure’, how confident are you that you could try this plan in the next week?”[^1_5][^1_1]
2. If <7, elicit “what would raise the number” (evokes problem‑solving from the client):
    - “What would need to happen to move that from a 4 to a 6?”[^1_1]
3. Translate those answers into small implementation details (time, cue, context), keeping syntax short:
    - “So, you’ll set a phone reminder for 9:15, and at that reminder you’ll open the report and work for 5 minutes.”
4. End with a commitment reflection rather than a directive:
    - “You’ve decided that tomorrow at 9:15 you’ll open the report and work on it for 5 minutes so you can feel more on top of things.”[^1_1]

***

### 6. Consolidate and externalize the goal (scaffolding for EF/WMD)

Purpose: create prompts outside working memory and strengthen sense of agency.

1. Ask permission to write down the plan in simple bullet points; ideally do it together on one page or card.[^1_4]
2. Read it back verbatim and ask the client to paraphrase once in their own words (retrieval practice).[^1_4]
3. Agree on one environmental cue and one accountability structure (e.g., brief check‑in, visual checklist).[^1_4]
4. Close with a strengths‑based reflection:
    - “You’ve come up with a plan that fits the way your brain works and moves you toward being the reliable colleague you care about being.”[^1_10][^1_1]

***

## Evidence for key MI components

### Open questions

- MI manuals and consensus guidance treat open questions as one element of OARS, emphasizing that they should be balanced by reflective listening and used to invite narrative, not interrogate.[^1_11][^1_2]
- Training and fidelity work consistently shows that a higher ratio of reflections to questions predicts better client outcomes; guidance from national MI materials recommends “ask one open question, then follow with two or more reflections,” indicating open questions are effective when embedded in reflective sequences rather than used in rapid succession.[^1_11][^1_2]
- Mechanism studies coding therapist language find that open questions that explicitly seek change talk (e.g., “How would you like things to be different?”) increase client change‑talk utterances, which in turn predict behavior change.[^1_12][^1_7]


### Reflective listening

- Reflective listening is described as “basic to all four MI processes” (engaging, focusing, evoking, planning), and national guidance states that careful reflective listening builds therapeutic alliance and self‑exploration, both empirically linked to better outcomes.[^1_2][^1_1]
- Observational data show that higher reflection‑to‑question ratios and use of complex reflections are associated with more change talk and fewer resistance behaviors.[^1_12][^1_2]
- Reflections that selectively emphasize change talk (e.g., double‑sided reflections ending with the change side) increase the likelihood of subsequent client change talk.[^1_12][^1_2]


### Decisional balancing

- Classical “pros and cons” decisional balance (DB) worksheets are conceptually distinct from MI’s preferred “evocation of change talk.”[^1_6]
- A focused review notes that neutral DB can be contraindicated when the clinician aims to strengthen motivation for change, because giving equal attention to reasons against change can increase sustain talk.[^1_6]
- DB is recommended when:
    - The clinician wishes to maintain neutrality (e.g., mandated contexts, values clarification).
    - The client is pre‑decisional and you explicitly frame the exercise as exploration, not persuasion.[^1_5][^1_6]
- MI‑consistent practice now often modifies DB to be asymmetrical: more depth on the “good things about change” and shorter, validating acknowledgement of what the person would miss.[^1_6][^1_2]

## Adaptations for working memory deficits and executive dysfunction

MI has been explicitly described as adaptable to cognitive impairment in rehabilitation populations, with emphasis on modifying delivery while retaining “spirit and skills.”[^1_4]

### Structural adaptations

- **Shorter, more frequent sessions**: Reduce duration and narrow focus to one behavior or goal per session to limit cognitive load.[^1_4]
- **Tighter focusing**: Move more quickly through engagement to a clear, singular focus; avoid broad life‑review when EF is limited.[^1_4]
- **Simplified language**: Use short, concrete sentences and avoid multi‑clause questions; check understanding frequently.[^1_4]


### Process adaptations during goal elicitation

1. Minimize concurrent demands
    - Avoid asking for multiple examples in one question (“Tell me about yesterday and last week”) and instead chunk (“Tell me about yesterday’s workday”).[^1_2][^1_4]
2. Increase externalization
    - Use visible aids (whiteboard, paper, tablet) to write key phrases as they are said: current situation, what matters, “one small change,” the goal.[^1_4]
    - Use simple icons or color‑coding to distinguish “now” vs “want.”
3. More frequent summaries
    - Offer brief “micro‑summaries” every few minutes: “So far I’ve heard… Did I miss anything important?” This compensates for forgetting earlier parts of the conversation.[^1_1][^1_4]
4. Constrained choice menus
    - Rather than “What goals do you have?”, offer 2–3 options explicitly linked to their statements, then ask “or something else?” to preserve autonomy:
        - “Based on what you said, we could focus on starting work tasks, managing emails, or taking a break at midday. Which feels like the best place to start?”[^1_1][^1_4]
5. Micro‑goals and “first step” emphasis
    - People with EF problems benefit from goals framed as very small, first, concrete actions (e.g., “open the document,” “lay out clothes”) rather than abstract outcomes (e.g., “be more organized”).[^1_13][^1_4]
    - When they propose a broad goal, explicitly ask: “What would be the very first visible thing that would tell you you’re doing that?”
6. Environmental and social scaffolding
    - Integrate prompts into the goal itself (alarms, visual schedules, checklists, task breakdowns), treating them as legitimate change strategies rather than “crutches.”[^1_14][^1_4]
    - Encourage involving a supporter (family, job coach) to review the one‑page goal summary between sessions.

# Goal Attainment Scaling: clinical methods, psychometric properties, and

practitioner use for decomposing vague objectives into measurable goals.
Evidence in rehabilitation and cognitive disability contexts. NOT general
outcome measurement literature. Specific protocols for goal negotiation
between practitioner and patient.

Goal Attainment Scaling (GAS) is a structured clinical method for collaboratively transforming vague priorities into individualized, measurable goals, with an established (though heterogeneous) evidence base in rehabilitation and cognitive disability contexts.[^2_1][^2_2]

***

## Core clinical method: how to do GAS

Most contemporary rehabilitation implementations derive from Kiresuk \& Sherman’s original method, operationalized in Turner‑Stokes’ practical guides.[^2_3][^2_2][^2_1]

### 1. Preparation and domain identification

- Clarify the purpose: evaluation of progress on personally meaningful goals over a defined interval (e.g., programme, 6–12 weeks).[^2_1][^2_3]
- Identify broad priority domains by semi‑structured interview with patient (and carer when relevant): e.g., self‑care, mobility, cognition, communication, work, participation.[^2_4][^2_5][^2_1]
- Select a small number of priority goals (often 1–3 in cognitive rehab; up to 5 in broader rehab) to prevent dilution and excessive burden.[^2_5][^2_3][^2_1]


### 2. Formulating each GAS goal

For each priority area, you formulate **one GAS “scale”** with 5 levels, anchored around the expected outcome (“0”).[^2_2][^2_3][^2_1]

1. Define the overall goal statement
    - Use SMART criteria but in plain language; e.g., “Manage my medication independently each morning.”[^2_4][^2_3][^2_1]
2. Specify the **time frame**
    - E.g., “By the end of the 12‑week cognitive rehabilitation programme.”[^2_3][^2_1]
3. Operationalize the 5 outcome levels as mutually exclusive, observable descriptions:[^2_2][^2_1][^2_3]
    - +2: Much more than expected
    - +1: Somewhat better than expected
    - 0: Expected level of outcome
    - −1: Somewhat less than expected
    - −2: Much less than expected / baseline

Example (TBI, medication management):[^2_1][^2_3]
    - −2: Needs verbal prompts and physical help daily to take correct medication.
    - −1: Needs verbal prompts most days; may manage dose with check from carer.
    - 0: Independently takes correct medication with use of pillbox and phone alarm on >5 of 7 days.
    - +1: Independently takes correct medication with a pillbox only on all 7 days.
    - +2: Independently manages medication on all 7 days and orders repeat prescriptions on time.
4. Check scale quality
    - Levels reflect increments on a **single dimension** (no mixing frequency, intensity, and quality in one scale).[^2_2][^2_1]
    - Only one level should be true at follow‑up; avoid overlapping statements.[^2_3][^2_1]
    - The “0” level is **realistic but challenging**, agreed by patient and team.[^2_1][^2_3]

### 3. Negotiating goals with patient (and carer)

Protocols stress that GAS is *not* a top‑down prescription but a negotiation process.[^2_6][^2_5][^2_3][^2_1]

- Use semi‑structured interview to elicit what is important and problematic for the individual; examples include Bangor Goal Setting Interview, Canadian Occupational Performance Measure, or bespoke interviews.[^2_7][^2_1]
- Translate patient language into draft GAS goals, then read them back and refine collaboratively until the patient endorses them as meaningful and acceptable.[^2_6][^2_5]
- Where team‑driven goals are necessary (e.g., safety, discharge criteria), explicitly mark them and still present them in accessible language.[^2_3][^2_1]
- Multi‑disciplinary meetings can be used to refine wording and ensure realistic expectations.[^2_1][^2_3]

Patient/provider qualitative studies note that patients value GAS when the process feels like a **joint venture** with compromise on both sides and when scaled outcomes clarify “small steps” rather than all‑or‑nothing change.[^2_6]

### 4. Weighting and scoring

1. Optional weighting
    - Each goal may be given a weight for **importance** and sometimes **difficulty**, typically on a 0–3 or 0–4 scale; these weights are then used to calculate an overall T‑score.[^2_4][^2_2][^2_1]
    - Weighting can be driven by patient (importance) and team (difficulty), or jointly negotiated.[^2_4][^2_3]
2. Assessment and scoring
    - At review, clinician determines which level (−2 to +2) best matches observed/ reported outcome for each goal.[^2_3][^2_1]
    - For group/programme evaluation, Kiresuk’s T‑score formula produces a standardized score (mean 50, SD 10) across multiple goals.[^2_2][^2_1]
    - If many patients consistently score <50, goals were probably over‑ambitious; >50 suggests overly easy goals.[^2_3]
3. Handling unexpected patterns
    - Guidelines recommend pre‑defining procedures if improvement occurs outside pre‑specified levels (e.g., very different type of achievement), often by documenting narrative explanation and, if feasible, mapping to nearest category.[^2_8][^2_1]

***

## Psychometric properties in rehabilitation and cognitive disability

### General rehabilitation and clinimetrics

- Literature reviews conclude that GAS is best conceptualised as a family of **methodologies** rather than a single standardized scale, which complicates psychometric evaluation.[^2_2][^2_1]
- Across rehabilitation contexts, GAS is described as **responsive** to change and able to detect clinically meaningful improvements that may be missed by generic measures.[^2_9][^2_1][^2_2]
- Studies have reported acceptable inter‑rater reliability when clinicians receive structured training in goal definition and use standardized rating procedures.[^2_1][^2_2]
- The method shows good **content validity** and patient‑centredness, because goals are idiographic and directly tied to personal priorities.[^2_6][^2_1]


### Cognitive rehabilitation and cognitive impairment

- Early cognitive rehabilitation work found that GAS could quantify clinically important change in patients undergoing cognitive rehabilitation, including those with TBI and other acquired brain injury.[^2_10][^2_5]
- A prospective observational study of 48 adults with acquired brain injury in two cognitive rehab programmes reported GAS was feasible to implement, allowing involvement of patients and caregivers and representation of multiple domains within the goal‑setting procedure.[^2_5]
- A literature‑based review and a recent educational review emphasize that GAS appears suitable and responsive in cognitive rehabilitation but highlight variability in quality, making training and adherence to structured procedures critical.[^2_8][^2_2][^2_1]
- In a multimodal cognitive and physical rehabilitation programme for people with mild dementia, GAS captured high goal attainment (93.3% increased goal attainment) and aligned with changes in conventional cognitive, functional, and behavioural measures.[^2_11]
- A systematic review and meta‑analysis of GAS in older adults with neurodegenerative diseases concluded that GAS is **reliable, feasible, and responsive** in populations with dementia and other cognitive impairments, though overall risk of bias was moderate due to small samples and heterogeneous methods.[^2_12]
- A recent psychosocial intervention trial for people with dementia and their carers used GAS both as outcome measure and as a process tool; analysis showed GAS could represent diverse, personally meaningful priorities, supporting person‑centred dementia care.[^2_13]


### Cognitive remediation / executive dysfunction contexts

- Modified GAS has been piloted in a cognitive remediation context for women in residential substance use treatment (a population with demonstrated executive and cognitive difficulties); a goal‑menu‑based, calculator‑supported version showed feasibility, reliability, and aspects of content and construct validity.[^2_14][^2_15]
- Goal attainment has been used as an outcome indicator in individually tailored, home‑based cognitive rehabilitation after traumatic brain injury; high rates of goal attainment were reported, with self‑reported executive dysfunction among factors associated with outcomes, underscoring GAS’s relevance to EF‑linked interventions.[^2_16]

## Using GAS to decompose vague objectives into measurable goals

Clinically, the “decomposition” function of GAS is built into the requirement to define 5 levels around a single core goal.[^2_1][^2_3]

### Stepwise decomposition method

1. Start from a vague priority
    - Example: “I want to be more independent.”
2. Clarify functional aspect and context via focused questioning
    - “In your day, when does being more independent matter most?” → “Getting dressed in the morning.”[^2_5][^2_4]
3. Agree the core goal statement
    - “Dress myself in the morning with less help.”
4. Identify **baseline** (−2) collaboratively
    - Ask patient/carer what currently happens; observe where possible; write a concrete description (e.g., “Needs full assistance for upper and lower body dressing every morning.”).[^2_5][^2_3]
5. Define “expected” (0) by negotiating a realistic but meaningful improvement
    - Integrate knowledge of prognosis, therapy input, and patient priorities; e.g., “By discharge, manage upper‑body dressing independently with set‑up help only, on most days.”[^2_3][^2_1]
6. Define intermediate and superior levels
    - −1: some improvement but short of 0 (e.g., “Requires less physical assistance, but still needs verbal cueing and help on most days”).
    - +1 and +2: “stretch” achievements clearly better than expected (e.g., “Independently dresses upper body on all days, lower body on most days” [+1]; “Fully independent dressing on all days” [+2]).[^2_1][^2_3]
7. Check each level against SMART and mutual exclusivity criteria
    - Single dimension, measurable indicators, specific time frame, agreed by patient and team.[^2_4][^2_3][^2_1]

In practice, clinicians often draft the scale and then revise it with the patient, rewriting wording to match the patient’s own phrasing and ensuring acceptability.[^2_6][^2_5]

***

## Specific protocols for practitioner–patient goal negotiation

Guides and empirical reports converge on several procedural elements.

### Protocol elements (Turner‑Stokes and others)

Turner‑Stokes’ “GAS in Rehabilitation: A Practical Guide” and subsequent educational review outline a clinical protocol built around five steps.[^2_8][^2_3][^2_1]

1. **Identify goals**
    - Interview patient (and family/carer) to identify problem areas and priority domains, within a set time frame and service context.[^2_3]
    - Encourage patient to describe what they want to be able to do, not what professionals want to measure.[^2_6][^2_1]
2. **Set SMART goals and timeframe**
    - Define expected level (0) and timeframe, with explicit negotiation of what is realistic and important.[^2_8][^2_3]
    - Use patient‑rated “importance” to guide which goals are prioritised and weighted.[^2_4][^2_3]
3. **Define outcome levels (−2 to +2)**
    - Develop detailed, observable descriptors for each level, checking wording with the patient.[^2_1][^2_3]
    - Record goals in accessible language and format; some services provide the patient a printed or digital goal sheet.[^2_5][^2_6]
4. **Review and adjust**
    - At interim points, goals and levels may be adjusted in discussion with the patient if rehabilitation trajectories or priorities have changed, while documenting rationale for modifications.[^2_8][^2_1]
5. **Score and reflect**
    - At the agreed review point, assign level for each goal; share results with patient and use them to structure discussion of progress and future priorities.[^2_6][^2_1]

Qualitative work with older adults and providers emphasizes that GAS‑based sessions often feel like **structured shared decision‑making**, helping clarify what “better” looks like and what support is needed, while also sometimes adding a sense of accountability that must be handled sensitively (e.g., not imposing rigid timelines where distress or fluctuating health interfere).[^2_6]

### Adaptations in cognitive and executive dysfunction

Across ABI, dementia, and cognitive remediation studies and guidance:[^2_15][^2_12][^2_5][^2_1]

- Use shorter interviews, focusing on 1–3 goals; avoid overloading with multiple scales.
- Involve carers to help articulate priorities and feasible levels while still privileging the person’s own preferences.
- Use visual aids (e.g., “ladder” diagrams for −2 to +2) and simple, concrete language.
- Ensure the −2 and 0 levels reflect observed behaviour and realistic change rather than idealized outcomes.
- For executive dysfunction, explicitly break long‑term aims into subgoals embedded in the GAS levels (e.g., “learn to use smartphone alarm” as a step toward employment‑related punctuality).[^2_8][^2_1]

# Supported decision-making frameworks in clinical psychology and disability

practice. Structured approaches for assisting individuals with executive
or cognitive impairments to identify and communicate preferences. Distinguish
from substituted judgment and guardianship models. Peer-reviewed or
practitioner-published. Prioritize clinical technique over legal analysis.

Supported decision-making (SDM) in disability and clinical practice is a structured way of helping people with cognitive or executive impairments understand options, weigh them, and communicate a choice, while the person themselves remains the decision-maker. It is explicitly distinguished from substituted judgment and guardianship, where another person makes decisions on the individual’s behalf.[^3_1][^3_2][^3_3][^3_4]

***

## Conceptual stance and distinction from guardianship

- SDM assumes decision-making capacity is task‑ and time‑specific and can often be *enhanced* with support (e.g., repetition, visual aids, trusted supporters), rather than being globally present or absent.[^3_5][^3_6][^3_1]
- In SDM, the individual retains legal authority; supporters help them *understand, deliberate, and express* their will and preferences but do not make the decision.[^3_2][^3_3][^3_4]
- Guardianship or substituted judgment shifts decision authority to a guardian, who then uses substituted‑judgment or best‑interests standards to decide for the person.[^3_4][^3_7][^3_2]
- SDM frameworks in mental health and cognitive disability sit conceptually close to shared decision making, but focus more explicitly on scaffolding cognitive processes and communication for people at the “margins of autonomy.”[^3_8][^3_9][^3_2]

***

## La Trobe Support for Decision‑Making Practice Framework (Douglas \& Bigby)

This is the most fully articulated evidence‑based framework specifically for supporting people with cognitive disabilities (ABI, intellectual disability, dementia).[^3_10][^3_11][^3_12][^3_13]

### Core principles

The framework synthesizes systematic reviews and multiple grounded‑theory studies with people with cognitive disabilities and their supporters. It emphasises:[^3_11][^3_12][^3_10]

- **Knowing the person** (history, communication, values, preferred supporters).[^3_12][^3_10]
- **Maximising understanding** of the specific decision by adapting information and environment.[^3_10][^3_12]
- **Supporting expression of will and preferences** in multiple modalities.[^3_8][^3_10]
- **Negotiating roles** and boundaries between supporter and decision‑maker.[^3_12][^3_10]


### High‑level practice steps

Summarising the La Trobe practice framework:[^3_13][^3_11][^3_10][^3_12]

1. **Prepare**
    - Clarify the decision to be made (scope, timing, consequences).
    - Identify who the person trusts and wants involved as supporters.
2. **Connect and understand the person**
    - Explore how they usually communicate yes/no, preferences, distress.
    - Gather knowledge of their values, past choices, routines, and goals.
3. **Tailor information**
    - Present options using simple language, chunked information, repetition, and multimodal supports (pictures, objects, written key points) as needed for executive and memory difficulties.[^3_14][^3_1]
    - Limit options in each discussion (e.g., 2–3 at a time) and check comprehension with “teach‑back.”[^3_6][^3_1][^3_14]
4. **Support deliberation**
    - Help the person explore pros/cons in their own terms, linking options to their stated values and everyday implications.[^3_10][^3_8]
    - Pace the discussion, allow extra processing time, and use breaks for people with reduced working memory or processing speed.[^3_15][^3_1]
5. **Support expression of a choice**
    - Provide scaffolds to express a decision: yes/no formats, choice boards, rating scales, or helping them phrase decisions in their own words.[^3_1][^3_10]
    - Check consistency over time and across contexts where there is concern about undue influence or fluctuating capacity.[^3_6][^3_8]
6. **Implement and review**
    - Document the person’s decision and the supports used.
    - Revisit the decision if circumstances change, emphasising that preferences can evolve.[^3_12][^3_10]

This framework is explicitly intended for clinical and disability practice (not just legal contexts) and is being piloted with direct support workers and health professionals.[^3_11][^3_10]

***

## Clinical techniques for executive / cognitive impairment

Several clinical and guidance papers describe concrete techniques to scaffold decision‑making abilities in mild cognitive impairment, dementia, ABI, and serious mental illness.[^3_15][^3_2][^3_1][^3_6]

### 1. Task analysis and tailoring to cognitive profile

- Capacity research emphasises differentiating components of decision‑making: understanding, appreciation, reasoning, and expressing a choice.[^3_5]
- Supported decision‑making papers propose using cognitive assessment (especially memory, language, executive function) to identify which components need support, then matching techniques: e.g., external memory aids in MCI, simplified reasoning aids in EF deficits.[^3_15][^3_1]


### 2. Information‑processing supports

Commonly recommended methods:[^3_2][^3_14][^3_1][^3_6]

- **Chunking and repetition**: present small amounts of information, repeat key points in consistent language, and summarise frequently.
- **Multimodal presentation**: combine spoken explanation with written bullet‑points, diagrams, pictures, or web‑based decision aids that incorporate values‑clarification exercises (e.g., for MCI intervention choices).[^3_16]
- **Timing and environment**: schedule discussions when the person is least fatigued, minimise distractions, and allow more time for processing.[^3_1][^3_6]

Interactive consent studies in serious mental illness show that repeated, interactive presentations and multimedia formats significantly improve decisional capacity compared with standard consent, supporting these methods as clinical tools.[^3_2]

### 3. Deliberation supports for executive dysfunction

For individuals with executive difficulties (initiation, shifting, working memory):[^3_6][^3_15][^3_1]

- **Stepwise comparison**: compare one option at a time rather than requiring simultaneous integration of multiple options.
- **Structured prompts**: ask consistent questions for each option (e.g., “What would be good? What might be hard?”), writing answers down as you go.
- **Decision grids/worksheets**: use simple tables with icons or keywords instead of long narrative text.
- **“Pause and return”**: break complex decisions across multiple shorter sessions, with brief recap and visual notes to bridge working‑memory gaps.

NICE guidance on decision‑making and mental capacity recommends exactly these types of supports—adjusted communication, environmental tailoring, and involvement of trusted supporters—before concluding that a person lacks capacity, including explicitly for people with executive dysfunction.[^3_6]

### 4. Communication and preference‑expression supports

Supported decision‑making frameworks emphasise accommodating diverse communication modes:[^3_14][^3_8][^3_10][^3_1]

- Use of aided communication (pictures, symbols, devices) for people with aphasia or limited verbal output.
- Observation of behavioural indicators of preference or distress when verbal articulation is limited, interpreted in light of the person’s history and routine.
- Encouraging supporters to help *interpret*, not to decide; clinicians are cautioned to guard against supporters speaking over the person or collapsing SDM into substituted decision‑making.[^3_8][^3_2]

***

## Practice models in serious mental illness and dementia

### Supported decision‑making in serious mental illness

- A review on SDM in serious mental illness notes that people with SMI frequently face guardianship or paternalistic decisions, despite evidence that their decisional capacity can be improved with cognitive and procedural supports.[^3_2]
- Recommended clinical strategies include: interactive consent procedures, plain‑language treatment options, structured shared decision‑making tools, and the use of supporters (family, peer specialists) to help the person articulate and remember their preferences.[^3_9][^3_2]
- The article contrasts SDM with guardianship, emphasising that SDM is voluntary, person‑directed, and revocable, whereas guardianship is imposed and shifts ultimate authority to the guardian.[^3_2]


### Supported decision‑making in MCI and dementia

- A recent paper on SDM in MCI and early dementia argues that many such individuals can still make their own decisions if provided with appropriate supports; it recommends tailored supports based on specific cognitive deficits (e.g., notes or summaries for episodic memory difficulties, simplified choices for EF deficits).[^3_15][^3_1]
- Web‑based decision aids for older adults with MCI have been user‑tested to support values clarification and option comparison, using design features such as minimal scrolling, pop‑up explanations, and user‑controlled pacing to accommodate cognitive limitations.[^3_16]

***

## High‑level frameworks and practitioner guides

Beyond La Trobe, several practice‑oriented frameworks and guides are relevant:

- A conceptual model of SDM for people with “dynamic impairments at the margins of autonomy” proposes a three‑step practice model: (1) creating enabling conditions and relationships; (2) eliciting and interpreting will and preferences; (3) translating these into concrete decisions and actions, while minimising coercion and undue influence.[^3_8]
- A “Diversity, Dignity, Equity and Best Practice” framework for SDM (Living with Disability Research Centre, La Trobe) articulates nine principles and eight essential practice elements, including recognition of legal capacity, commitment to relational autonomy, attention to power imbalances, and systematic support planning.[^3_13]
- National and regional good‑practice guides (e.g., mental health and disability commissions) translate these principles into checklists for clinicians: clarify the decision, maximise understanding, support communication, involve significant and trusted people, and only move to substitute decision‑making when all reasonable supports have been tried.[^3_17][^3_14][^3_6]

These documents are practice‑focused and designed for health and social care professionals rather than legal practitioners.[^3_17][^3_10][^3_6]

***

## Summary contrast: SDM vs substituted judgment / guardianship

| Aspect | Supported decision-making | Substituted judgment / guardianship |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Who decides? | Person with disability retains decision-making authority.[^3_3][^3_4] | Guardian / substitute decision-maker has legal authority to decide.[^3_2][^3_4][^3_7] |
| Role of supporters | Help understand, deliberate, and communicate choices; provide information and scaffolding.[^3_10][^3_1][^3_3] | Provide information, but ultimately decide according to substituted judgment or best interests standards.[^3_2][^3_4] |
| Legal status | Typically informal or contractual; revocable by the person; does not remove legal capacity.[^3_3][^3_4] | Court-ordered; alters or removes legal decision-making rights; revocation requires legal process.[^3_2][^3_4][^3_7] |
| Clinical focus | Enhancing autonomy, tailoring supports to cognitive profile, facilitating expression of will and preferences.[^3_10][^3_8][^3_1] | Protecting welfare when capacity judged absent despite supports; decisions often framed in risk/benefit and safety terms.[^3_2][^3_6] |

# Ecological momentary assessment for preference and intention capture in

clinical populations. How in-context, real-time elicitation differs from
retrospective goal-setting in accuracy and specificity of captured intent.
Applications in ADHD or executive dysfunction. Peer-reviewed or
practitioner-published.

Ecological momentary assessment (EMA) captures preferences, intentions, and antecedents *in situ* and in (near) real time, reducing recall and reconstruction biases that distort retrospective goal‑setting; this seems particularly relevant when executive and working‑memory functions are compromised, as in ADHD.[^4_1][^4_2][^4_3][^4_4]

***

## How EMA differs from retrospective goal‑setting

- EMA samples thoughts, feelings, context, and intended or actual behaviour multiple times per day, in natural environments, using prompts anchored to the current or very recent moment (for example, “Since the last prompt, did you want to…?”, “Right now, how strong is your intention to…?”).[^4_5][^4_6][^4_4]
- Retrospective goal‑setting and self‑report rely on the “remembering self”: people reconstruct experiences and preferences using heuristics, mood‑congruent memory, and lay theories, which often diverge substantially from momentary data.[^4_2][^4_3]
- EMA reduces these memory and reconstruction biases by asking for reports at or close to the time of experience; meta‑analytic and conceptual reviews highlight that symptoms and states are monitored “more accurately compared with retrospective reports,” even relative to end‑of‑day diaries.[^4_3][^4_4]
- In psychiatric outpatients, EMA‑derived indices of affective instability showed only modest agreement with questionnaire and retrospective ratings, indicating that global self‑reports can mischaracterise within‑day dynamics that matter for goal pursuit.[^4_2]

Clinically, this means EMA can reveal when and where intentions actually arise, fluctuate, and convert into actions, whereas retrospective goal‑setting tends to produce broad, context‑insensitive statements (e.g., “I want to exercise more”) that may not fit real‑world temporal and situational contingencies.[^4_6][^4_3]

***

## EMA for preference and intention capture

Although most EMA work in clinical populations has focused on symptoms, emotion, and behaviour, several strands illustrate how preference and intention can be elicited more precisely in context.

### Momentary intentions and proximal precursors

- EMA designs in adolescent and young‑adult mental health routinely ask about **current or near‑future intentions** (e.g., intentions to use cannabis or alcohol, to diet, binge, or exercise) immediately following exposure to stimuli or events; these in‑the‑moment intentions often show robust associations with proximal triggers that might be missed retrospectively.[^4_7][^4_8]
- An ecological mobile momentary intervention for goal pursuit used EMA prompts to repeatedly assess goal characteristics (reward, meaning, importance) and to estimate “the extent to which people are acting toward a goal, can visualize it, and feel self‑efficacious” in the flow of daily life.[^4_6]
- EMA‑driven feedback has been used to summarise patterns back to participants, with the explicit clinical aim of enhancing insight into their own preferences, patterns, and triggers, thereby refining subsequent goal‑setting.[^4_9][^4_3]

In all of these, in‑context EMA yields *fine‑grained profiles* of when certain intentions (e.g., to use a substance, to work on a task) are strongest, which situations support or undermine them, and how quickly they decay—information that is hard to recover accurately in session‑based retrospective interviews.[^4_4][^4_3][^4_6]

***

## EMA in ADHD and executive dysfunction: relevance to intent

ADHD and related executive dysfunction conditions have been a major focus for EMA work, albeit more on emotion and symptoms than on explicit goals; these studies nonetheless show why EMA is valuable for capturing intention and preference in these populations.

- Reviews and feasibility studies conclude that EMA is a valid and feasible method for capturing “real‑world and real‑time” data on emotional and behavioural functioning in ADHD, with good compliance in youth and adults.[^4_10][^4_11][^4_5][^4_1]
- EMA studies demonstrate substantial within‑day variability in attention, impulsivity, and emotion, often only weakly related to baseline trait measures; everyday cognitive control and emotional dysregulation show dynamic patterns that static testing misses.[^4_12][^4_13][^4_11]
- In adolescents treated for ADHD, 17 days of four‑times‑daily EMA showed that self‑rated ADHD symptoms fluctuated substantially and tended to decrease over the assessment period, suggesting that frequent in‑the‑moment ratings can both capture variability and possibly alter awareness and symptom appraisal.[^4_14]
- EMA of emotion in girls with ADHD showed strong feasibility and correlations between child and maternal ratings, supporting its use to track momentary states that can influence when intentions are likely to be enacted or abandoned.[^4_11][^4_10]

For executive functioning, EMA does not directly replace performance‑based “ecological” tasks, but it complements them by situating intention and self‑regulation in daily routines and contexts rather than laboratory settings. This is particularly important in ADHD, where real‑life executive deficits may be present despite near‑normal performance on standard tests.[^4_15][^4_12]

***

## Combined EMA–EMI designs targeting real‑time goals in ADHD

A small but growing literature uses EMA not just for assessment but as an active ingredient in ecological momentary interventions (EMIs) that target goal pursuit and self‑regulation.

- A recent mHealth EMI for young adults with ADHD and high‑risk alcohol use used EMA symptom monitoring as an intervention component to increase self‑awareness, alongside personalized feedback and “tips” tailored by EMA data.[^4_16][^4_9]
    - EMA questions were used to track current urges, context, and coping strategy use, which then triggered tailored prompts—effectively linking *momentary intentions* and *context* to concrete behavioural suggestions.[^4_9]
- An experimental EMI to support dynamic goal pursuit in adults (non‑clinical) combined EMA prompts (mood, motivation, goal reward/meaning, current progress) with MCII and COM‑B‑based micro‑interventions delivered in real time; the authors argue this approach “bolster[s] skill acquisition” for goal pursuit in the presence of competing priorities.[^4_6]

These designs illustrate how, in populations with executive dysfunction, EMA can be harnessed to (a) detect windows of opportunity for action (e.g., moments of higher motivation or lower distraction) and (b) deliver just‑in‑time scaffolds that help convert short‑lived intentions into specific, feasible actions.[^4_16][^4_9][^4_6]

***

## Why EMA is particularly useful for executive dysfunction

Taken together, EMA and EMA‑based interventions offer several advantages over purely retrospective goal‑setting for people with ADHD or executive impairment:

1. **Reduced reliance on working memory and global self‑theories**
    - Executive and memory impairments make it harder to integrate experiences across time and accurately summarise “what I’m like” or “what I want to do”; EMA shifts the emphasis to “what I’m experiencing and intending *right now*,” which is cognitively simpler and less distorted by recall biases.[^4_17][^4_3][^4_2]
2. **Capture of temporal and situational specificity**
    - EMA highlights when and where intentions to work, use substances, or enact coping strategies arise and lapse (for instance, after social media exposure or in particular social contexts), offering a rich map of context–intention–behaviour chains that can inform personalised goals and implementation intentions.[^4_8][^4_7][^4_6]
3. **Improved ecological validity of targets**
    - Studies in ADHD show that “everyday” cognitive control and emotional variability in EMA data can differ markedly from lab measures, implying that goals based only on clinic data risk missing the actual situations that drive impairment.[^4_12][^4_15][^4_11]
4. **Potential to enhance self‑awareness and metacognition**
    - Participants frequently report that EMA monitoring increases awareness of their own patterns; in ADHD and related conditions, this fostered awareness may itself be a mechanism for more realistic, self‑congruent goal‑setting.[^4_18][^4_14][^4_3][^4_9]
5. **Alignment with dynamic, micro‑goal approaches**
    - For clients whose intentions are highly labile, EMA‑linked EMI allows goals to be repeatedly refined and re‑anchored to current context, rather than relying on static plans set weeks earlier in clinic.[^4_5][^4_16][^4_6]

***

## Implications for clinical practice

While there is not yet a large body of trials explicitly comparing EMA‑anchored goal‑setting with traditional retrospective goal‑setting, convergent evidence from EMA vs retrospective measurement and from ADHD EMA studies suggests some practical translational steps:[^4_1][^4_4][^4_16][^4_2][^4_6]

- Use brief EMA (or EMA‑like diaries) before or between sessions to collect in‑the‑moment ratings of:
    - What the person wanted or intended to do.
    - Whether they did it.
    - Context (where, who with, time of day) and current affect / arousal.
- Summarise these data with the client to identify *where* intentions are strongest yet follow‑through is weakest, and use this as a basis for setting highly specific goals and implementation intentions.
- For ADHD/executive dysfunction, keep EMA prompts extremely short, reduce frequency if needed, and consider combining fixed‑time prompts with event‑based prompts (e.g., “when starting homework/work,” “when about to use a substance”) to target key decision points.[^4_16][^4_1][^4_6]
- Where possible, integrate EMA data into other structured goal‑methods (e.g., motivational interviewing, Goal Attainment Scaling) to enhance the ecological specificity of goals and to monitor micro‑progress between sessions.[^4_3][^4_6]

# Non-verbal and multi-modal elicitation methods for goals and preferences.

Visual, spatial, embodied, or artifact-based approaches to surfacing intent
without relying on verbal articulation. Evidence from occupational therapy,
design research, AAC research, and participatory design with neurodivergent
populations. Practitioner or peer-reviewed evidence.

Non‑verbal and multi‑modal methods can reliably elicit goals and preferences by leveraging visual, spatial, embodied, and artifact‑based expression rather than primarily spoken language, and there is a reasonably rich practice and research base across OT, AAC, and participatory design.[^5_1][^5_2][^5_3][^5_4][^5_5]

***

## Visual tools for eliciting preferences and goals

### Picture‑ and video‑based preference assessments (AAC / ID / autism)

- Behaviour‑analytic preference assessment work compares tangible, pictorial, verbal, and video modalities for identifying preferred items and activities in people with developmental disabilities.[^5_6][^5_7][^5_2][^5_8]
- Multiple Stimulus Without Replacement (MSWO) and paired‑stimulus formats have been implemented with photos, app‑icon images, and brief videos, often yielding hierarchies that correlate well with tangible assessments and predicting reinforcing efficacy.[^5_9][^5_8][^5_1]
- Discrimination‑skills studies show that the *appropriate modality* depends on the person’s visual and auditory‑visual matching abilities: some individuals respond reliably only to tangible or pictorial stimuli, whereas others can indicate stable preferences across verbal, pictorial, and video options.[^5_7][^5_2]

Clinical use:

- Lay out photos or simple line drawings of activities, environments, or people and invite the person to select, order, or group them (e.g., “things I like to do,” “places I want to go more,” “people I like to be with”), then treat the resulting arrays as raw material for goal‑setting.
- For autistic or minimally speaking clients, video‑based choice (short clips showing activities) may better convey complex, dynamic options (e.g., group vs solitary activity) than static photos.[^5_8][^5_9]


### Visual scene displays and personalized imagery (AAC)

- Visual scene displays (VSDs) present rich scenes (photos or illustrations) with embedded hotspots; they are widely used in AAC to support communication about familiar contexts.[^5_5]
- Personalization of VSDs (images depicting the person and familiar partners/places) increases engagement and appears to support more effective communication compared to generic scenes.[^5_5]

Clinical use:

- Build VSDs around contexts that are potential goal targets (e.g., workplace, classroom, community setting) and invite the person to point to or activate hotspots corresponding to preferred activities, routines, or role expectations.
- Use co‑construction of VSDs (choosing photos together, marking important elements) as a way to surface what the person values in those contexts.[^5_5]


### Visual mapping and diagrammatic methods

- A systematic review of visual communication methods with older adults (including those with cognitive impairments) describes use of photographs, pictograms, and graphical timeline or map‑based tools to elicit thoughts, feelings, and preferences regarding daily life and health care.[^5_3]
- Visual tools were used to support reflection, side‑by‑side comparison of options, and identification of what mattered most; participants and carers reported that visuals “broke up textual monotony” and facilitated reasoning and preference expression.[^5_3]

Examples relevant to goal‑elicitation:

- **Life Story Board**: a physical board and magnet/icon system used as a visual interview tool; school counsellors found it feasible for helping young people externalize life domains, stressors, and hopes by arranging icons on a board, which then anchored conversations about goals.[^5_10]
- **Visual maps / habit‑maps** in dementia: visual maps supporting routines and choices have been developed as assistive technology to scaffold engagement and indicate preferred activities for people with Alzheimer’s disease and related dementias.[^5_11]

## Spatial and artifact‑based elicitation in occupational therapy

### Embodied and environmental mapping

- OT and rehab studies using technology‑aided spatial cues and preferred stimulation show that arranging objects and environmental cues around a space (e.g., different stations with preferred items, each paired with specific prompts) can reveal preferences and support occupational engagement without relying on verbal explanation.[^5_12][^5_13]
- Participants with intellectual disability and visual impairment learned to navigate between stations and choose preferred stimulation (music, video, etc.), with engagement measured behaviourally; selection patterns served as a proxy for preference and informed individualized occupational goals.[^5_13]

Clinical use:

- Set up an “activity circuit” (stations with different tasks or sensory experiences) and allow the person to move freely; record which stations they approach, stay at, or return to, and use this as evidence about preferred occupations or environments.
- Combine with simple symbols or colours at each station to build a shared visual code for later goal discussions.


### Art‑based, play‑ and object‑mediated methods

- Art therapy and creative OT/play‑therapy are described as non‑directive methods that use drawing, collage, construction, and multimedia to let “beneficiaries create their own images with which they want to interact [and] arrange their environment,” thereby surfacing concerns, interests, and self‑concept without direct questioning.[^5_14][^5_15]
- These approaches are used to visualize experiences and desires, offering raw material for later, more structured goal negotiation (e.g., what themes recur in drawings, what environments or roles are depicted).[^5_15][^5_14]

Clinical use:

- Ask clients to draw or build “a good day,” “a place where you feel like yourself,” or “what you wish life looked like in a year,” then jointly annotate the artifact with minimal text or icons to identify implicit goals (e.g., more time outdoors, fewer people, particular sensory elements).

## Participatory and co‑design with neurodivergent people

### Adaptive interview and co‑design methods

- A recent HCI systematic review and case‑study paper on designing assistive technologies with neurodivergent users highlights that traditional verbal, question‑and‑answer interviews often fail to elicit rich preferences.[^5_4]
- The authors demonstrate adaptive interview methods that integrate visual aids, interactive sketching, and hands‑on activities (e.g., arranging cards, building low‑fidelity prototypes) to help autistic and other neurodivergent participants express needs and desires more concretely.[^5_4]
- Participatory design and co‑design were widely used, with benefits including deeper understanding of user requirements, more realistic expectations, and empowerment of marginalized users.[^5_16][^5_17][^5_4]

Clinical use:

- Use **probing interviews with materials**: while talking, invite the person to sketch interfaces, arrange feature cards (“notifications,” “quiet mode,” “timers”), or build mock‑ups with sticky notes; interpret arrangements and modifications as expressions of preference and priorities.[^5_4]
- Offer multiple representation options (draw, choose stickers, move objects) in parallel with or instead of verbal explanation, especially for autistic or ADHD clients who find verbal meta‑reflection difficult.


### XR and embodied participatory design

- XR participatory design work with autistic learners emphasizes iterative co‑creation and uses movement, gestures, and situated interaction in virtual environments to understand comfort levels, sensory preferences, and educational priorities.[^5_16]
- Design‑based research cycles rely heavily on observation of how participants interact with spatial and sensory aspects of prototypes, taking those behavioural patterns as data about preferences and tolerances rather than relying solely on verbal feedback.[^5_16]

Clinical implication:

- For clients with strong embodied/sensory preferences, using VR/AR or low‑tech spatial simulations (mock‑up workspaces, classroom layouts) can help identify preferred spatial arrangements, social distances, and sensory conditions that then translate into environmental or occupational goals.

## Multi‑modal preference assessment in AAC and ID

AAC literature offers structured, evidence‑based methods for assessing preferences using non‑verbal modalities:

- Studies comparing pictorial, verbal, and tangible preference assessments show that when basic visual and matching discriminations are present, pictorial and video‑based assessments can validly substitute for tangible choices, and that different modalities may be optimal depending on an individual’s discrimination profile.[^5_2][^5_6][^5_7][^5_8]
- Tablet‑based MSWO assessments using app icons demonstrate that digital, picture‑based formats can efficiently identify preferred activities for children with autism, with good correspondence to traditional methods.[^5_1]

Clinical use:

- Start with an assessment of discrimination skills (object–picture and picture–object matching; receptive vocabulary) to choose the modality; then use choice‑based arrays (touch, eye‑gaze, reaching) to elicit stable preference hierarchies that inform goal priorities (e.g., which communication topics, leisure activities, or partners to emphasise).[^5_6][^5_7][^5_1]

## Putting it together: technique themes across fields

Across OT, AAC, and participatory design with neurodivergent populations, several **common design principles** emerge:[^5_13][^5_3][^5_4][^5_5]

- **Externalization**: Move intent and preference “out of the head” into visible forms—pictures, diagrams, physical layouts, or digital scenes—so that the person can manipulate and reflect on them without heavy verbal or working‑memory demands.
- **Personalization**: Use personally meaningful imagery (their own environments, people, and objects) rather than generic clip‑art, which improves engagement and clarity of what is being chosen or endorsed.[^5_13][^5_5]
- **Choice through action**: Let people express goals and preferences through selection, arrangement, and modification of artifacts (choosing, ordering, clustering, editing), with verbal language used mainly to label or lightly interpret what they have already done.[^5_10][^5_12][^5_4]
- **Scaffolded interpretation**: Practitioners treat non‑verbal outputs (drawings, spatial layouts, repeated station visits, selection patterns) as hypotheses about underlying preferences and *check* them with the person where possible, rather than imposing unilateral meanings.[^5_3][^5_4]
