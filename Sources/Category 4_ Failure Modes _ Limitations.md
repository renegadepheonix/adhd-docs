# Documented failure modes of structured goal-setting protocols in ADHD

and autistic populations. Empirical evidence, clinical literature, or
practitioner-documented patterns. Specific cognitive mechanisms implicated:
working memory load, temporal discounting, ambiguity intolerance, demand
sensitivity. Prioritize studies and practitioner-documented protocols
over opinion content.

Structured goal-setting often fails for ADHD and autistic people when protocols assume intact working memory, stable prospective memory, low temporal discounting, high tolerance for ambiguity, and low demand sensitivity; these assumptions are repeatedly violated in empirical and clinical literature. Below is a synthesis around your four mechanisms, focusing on documented failure modes rather than generic “executive dysfunction.”[^1_1][^1_2][^1_3][^1_4][^1_5][^1_6][^1_7]

## Working memory load and goal neglect

- Adults with higher inattention show pronounced **goal** neglect on tasks that require maintaining and integrating multi-step verbal instructions in working memory, even when they can repeat the rules; they fail at actually implementing the rule set, consistent with “lost in the middle” of goal plans.[^1_4]
- In adult ADHD, complex prospective memory tasks show impairments in task planning, plan recall, and self-initiation, with patients remembering intentions but failing to execute them at the right time/context, i.e., the implementation gap rather than goal formation per se.[^1_2]
- A review on organizational skills in ADHD argues that multisetting organizational impairment arises in large part from working-memory-related failure to anticipate, plan, and maintain goal-directed actions, especially when tasks require coordinating multiple sub-goals, materials, and time frames.[^1_1]
- Naturalistic videogame assessment (EPELI) of adult ADHD shows reduced goal-directed action and poorer prospective memory when participants must keep several instructions in mind and self-organize action sequences in an everyday-like environment, again pointing to failure at holding/using multiple intentions rather than at initial goal agreement.[^1_3]

Implication for protocols:

- Multi-step SMART plans, layered action plans, or multi-goal worksheets that rely on the person holding several contingencies (if X, then Y, then Z) in mind are likely to fail in translation to behavior, even when clients can state the plan and endorse it.[^1_2][^1_4][^1_1]
- Implementation-intention-type strategies are vulnerable when each intention adds to working memory demands (e.g., several “if–then” cues in one day), leading to intention overload and subsequent goal neglect rather than protection.[^1_4]


## Temporal discounting, delayed contingencies, and long-horizon goals

- ADHD is consistently associated with steeper temporal discounting (stronger preference for smaller-sooner over larger-later rewards), which undermines adherence to long-range goals whose payoffs are temporally distant and abstract (e.g., savings, studying, health behavior change); this shows up experimentally in choice tasks and functionally in poorer long-term academic and occupational attainment despite stated goals.[^1_8][^1_1][^1_2]
- Prospective memory studies in adult ADHD show that when reward or feedback is delayed and not embedded in the current task context, execution of intended actions drops substantially, even though intentions were encoded and could be recalled when prompted.[^1_3][^1_2]

Implication for protocols:

- Standard goal-setting frameworks that emphasize distal outcomes (e.g., 6‑ or 12‑month SMART goals; ideal future self exercises) without dense, immediate reinforcement structures often see sharp drop-off after initial sessions, with behavior reverting to short-term preference patterns.[^1_1][^1_2]
- Protocols that confine reward to “completion of the full plan” (e.g., only after full week compliance) are structurally misaligned with high temporal discounting and tend to produce all‑or‑nothing adherence, oscillating between “on track” and “total abandonment.”[^1_2][^1_1]


## Ambiguity, uncertainty, and goal formulation in autism

- Reviews of intentional and cognitive control in autism note that autistic individuals often show relatively intact basic rule-following under tightly specified conditions but difficulties with flexible goal setting, shifting between goals, and dealing with under-specified or conflicting demands, especially in more naturalistic tasks.[^1_5]
- Intolerance of uncertainty (IU) is highly elevated in autistic populations and strongly linked to anxiety and avoidance; situations with unclear contingencies or partially defined expectations provoke high cognitive and emotional load.[^1_9][^1_10][^1_7]
- IU accounts for a substantial proportion of variance in anxiety in autistic adults; sensory sensitivity interacts with IU to amplify distress in unpredictable contexts, increasing avoidance of new or loosely defined activities.[^1_7]

Implication for protocols:

- Generic “values exploration” or open-ended “what do you want to work on?” can stall because the task itself is high-uncertainty and requires abstract prospection and weighing of loosely specified options, triggering anxiety and shutdown/avoidance.[^1_5][^1_7]
- SMART-style goal worksheets that leave key elements underspecified (who, where, supports, environmental conditions) may be experienced as ambiguous and unsafe, leading to apparent “non-engagement” despite motivation to improve functioning.[^1_7][^1_5]
- Autistic co-design work on goal tools found that standard occupational/rehab goal-setting approaches did not provide enough concrete, visual, and context-specific prompts; the resulting picture-based card-sort tool was explicitly developed to scaffold initial goal identification and prioritization because many users struggled to generate goals from open questions.[^1_6]


## Demand sensitivity, control, and protocol-driven pressure

- In autistic populations, clinical and qualitative literatures on anxiety and emotion regulation emphasize that high external demands, especially when coupled with uncertainty or low control, exacerbate distress and avoidance; IU interventions (e.g., CUES) had to be parent-mediated and graded to be tolerated.[^1_10][^1_9]
- Adaptations of DBT for autistic individuals stress the need for collaborative, interest-based, and visually supported structures; rigid diary-card demands and high-frequency self-monitoring can trigger resistance, dropout, or superficial compliance without genuine skills practice.[^1_11]
- In ADHD, heavy self-monitoring and diary-based behavioral interventions in physiotherapy and other domains show initial behavioral change that is not maintained; process evaluation indicates that the burden of diary completion, repeated self-report, and frequent goal review exceeded participants’ cognitive and motivational bandwidth, impairing sustained engagement.[^1_12]

Implication for protocols:

- High-demand, high-frequency self-monitoring (daily logs, detailed habit tracking) can act as a demand stimulus rather than a support, particularly when not co-designed and when feedback is delayed or purely evaluative.[^1_12][^1_11]
- Strongly clinician-led goals that do not allow the person to exercise control (choice of targets, pacing, format) are more likely to encounter “pseudo-agreement” in-session and then non-implementation between sessions, a pattern widely noted in practitioner literature on autism and ADHD interventions.[^1_6][^1_11][^1_12]


## Practitioner-documented adaptations and patterns

- A metacognitive work-performance intervention for adults with ADHD (Work-MAP) uses self-selected work goals and telehealth coaching; even in this optimized context, the trial emphasizes the need for highly individualized, self-tailored goal selection and graded implementation, recognizing that standard goal-setting and generic executive function training produce limited transfer to real-world work behavior.[^1_13]
- Participatory design of an autistic goal-setting card-sort tool explicitly responded to practitioner observations that standard goal-setting methods did not elicit usable goals and that clients often either fixated on detail-level problems or struggled to identify priorities; visual, concrete domains and limited choices improved engagement and clarity of priorities.[^1_6]
- IU-focused protocols for autistic children (CUES) conceptualize treatment targets not as classic performance goals (e.g., “attend X events”) but as graded exposures to uncertainty, acknowledging that directly setting performance targets in high-uncertainty domains often provoked avoidance and was not feasible without first changing the underlying cognitive-affective response to uncertainty.[^1_9][^1_10]

# Verbal working memory and language production as barriers to intent

articulation in executive dysfunction. How verbal self-report tasks
impose cognitive load that degrades the quality of expressed goals.
Implications for elicitation method design. Peer-reviewed cognitive
psychology and neuropsychology literature.

Verbal self-report of intentions and goals is a non-trivial executive task: it recruits verbal working memory, monitoring, and language production, and executive weakness in these systems degrades the precision and stability of articulated goals. This has direct implications for how we design elicitation methods in populations with executive dysfunction.[^2_1][^2_2][^2_3][^2_4]

## How verbal working memory and language burden goal articulation

- Neuropsychological work shows that language and executive systems are tightly interdependent: retaining semantic content while inhibiting irrelevant associations is an executive function demand as much as a linguistic one.[^2_3][^2_1]
- In semantic short‑term memory disorders, patients can understand and even recognize appropriate responses but fail to maintain relevant semantic information long enough to produce accurate verbal output, illustrating how limited-capacity verbal working memory distorts what gets said.[^2_1]
- Systematic reviews of developmental language disorder and related conditions find large deficits on verbal working memory tasks (e.g., backward digit/span, listening span), indicating reduced capacity to hold and manipulate verbal information while planning a response.[^2_2][^2_5]

In a goal-setting context, these same constraints mean that:

- Holding internal intentions, translating them into language, and keeping the emerging sentence in mind long enough to edit it on the fly is a high-load operation; as load rises, people simplify, omit contingencies, or shift to stock phrases (“do better,” “be more organized”).[^2_2][^2_1]
- When questions are complex or nested (“In the next six months, what specific, measurable…?”), part of the question is lost from working memory during formulation, biasing answers toward whichever fragment is most salient or easiest to verbalize.


## Executive control, fluency, and degradation of expressed goals

- Verbal fluency performance in neurologically typical adults is strongly predicted by executive functions such as shifting, inhibition, and working memory updating, not just by lexical store size.[^2_4]
- Executive dysfunction (e.g., in preterm children, ADHD, language disorders) is repeatedly associated with poorer verbal fluency, planning, and organization, reflecting difficulty initiating, switching, and monitoring verbal output.[^2_6][^2_3][^2_4][^2_2]
- Reviews of executive function in language-impaired populations emphasize that language tests often confound executive and linguistic demands; when executive control is weak, people may produce shorter, less structured, and more perseverative responses even if basic linguistic knowledge is intact.[^2_3][^2_2]

Applied to intent articulation:

- Open-ended, time-limited self-report (“Tell me your main goals”) functions much like an unstructured fluency task under social evaluation; those with executive weaknesses may default to generic, externally cued goals, under-specify conditions, or perseverate on one salient theme.[^2_4][^2_2]
- Monitoring and revising one’s own utterance in real time (e.g., “that’s not quite right; let me restate”) is executive-heavy; with limited monitoring capacity, initial drafts of goals remain uncorrected, so the recorded goal is a noisy approximation of internal intent.


## Cognitive load from verbal self-report procedures themselves

- Neuropsychological assessment literature notes that many “cognitive” screeners embed substantial language demands; instructions plus verbal response requirements can mask underlying abilities or inflate apparent deficits when linguistic and executive loads are high.[^2_7][^2_3]
- In experimental work, adding concurrent verbal tasks (articulatory suppression, complex span tasks) reliably disrupts planning, reasoning, and problem solving, demonstrating that verbal working memory resources are a shared pool for both thinking and speaking.[^2_1][^2_3]

For goal elicitation, this implies that:

- The more we require extended, fully verbalized, self-structured narratives of goals, the more we pull resources away from the internal planning we wish to access; the act of describing intentions can impair the underlying planning process in people with limited capacity.
- Multi-step verbal questionnaires that require remembering prior answers or instructions (“thinking about what you said earlier, now tell me…”) impose further working memory and monitoring demands, increasing drift between true intent and what ends up on paper.


## Design implications for elicitation methods

Drawing from these mechanisms, elicitation methods that respect verbal WM and executive limits tend to:

1. **Reduce online verbal load**
    - Use recognition and selection instead of pure generation where possible (e.g., goal menus, card sorts, visual lists), then refine verbally in smaller steps.[^2_7][^2_3]
    - Break questions into single, short prompts presented one at a time, keeping verbal instructions minimal and stable.
2. **Externalize memory and structure**
    - Provide persistent visual scaffolds (key domains, time frames, criteria) so the person does not have to hold the question structure in mind while formulating language.[^2_7][^2_3]
    - Let people sketch, circle, or rank options first, then use that external representation as the basis for brief verbal summaries, reducing reliance on internal sequencing.
3. **Decouple planning from full sentence production**
    - Separate the phase where people work out what they want (using visual tools, timelines, or checklists) from the phase where goals are translated into documentation language; the latter can be collaboratively “translated” by the practitioner with confirmation rather than produced wholly by the client.
    - Allow fragmentary or bullet-point responses; treat syntactic completeness as non-essential, focusing instead on capturing constraints, contexts, and preferences.
4. **Support iterative refinement rather than single-shot articulation**
    - Expect first-pass verbalizations to be rough; build in cycles of clarification using yes/no, forced-choice, or short follow-ups (“more about X or more about Y?”) that limit fresh generative demand.
    - Where executive dysfunction is prominent, use shorter sessions or multiple micro-elicitation points rather than one long, high-load interview.

# Temporal inconsistency and state-dependent preference reversals in ADHD

and executive dysfunction. How intention quality varies by cognitive state,
time pressure, emotional arousal, or task-switching context. Implications
for when and how to elicit goals. Peer-reviewed.

Intention quality in ADHD is notably state-dependent: temporal choices and plans vary with working memory load, emotional arousal, and task context, producing inconsistent preferences and fragile goal commitments. This has clear implications for when and how you elicit goals if you want them to be stable and implementable.[^3_1][^3_2][^3_3][^3_4][^3_5]

## Temporal inconsistency and decision instability in ADHD

- A meta-analysis shows that people with ADHD, on average, discount delayed rewards more steeply than controls, but also highlights substantial variability in delay discounting across tasks and contexts, suggesting that temporal preferences are less stable and more susceptible to situational factors.[^3_4]
- A newer study found ADHD symptoms were associated not just with a bias toward immediate rewards but with *inconsistent* temporal choices—participants with higher ADHD traits sometimes chose the delayed option when it was better and sometimes when it was worse, showing higher intra-individual variability in intertemporal choice.[^3_2][^3_1]
- This inconsistency in temporal decision making appears to reflect suboptimal and unstable value integration rather than a simple, fixed preference for immediacy, which maps onto clinical observations that goal commitments made in one context are not reliably honored in others.[^3_1][^3_2]

Implication: a goal articulated at one time point may reflect a transient valuation state rather than a stable preference structure, especially for long-horizon goals.

## Cognitive load, task switching, and intention quality

- Dual-task work with children with ADHD shows that adding working memory load (e.g., concurrent digit repetition) during delay-discounting tasks increases impulsive choices, indicating that limited cognitive resources push decisions toward simpler, immediate options.[^3_3]
- When cognitive load is high, ADHD groups show more variable and less optimal temporal decision making compared with controls, suggesting that working memory demand degrades consistency of preferences over time.[^3_5][^3_3]
- Studies manipulating cognitive load and motivational contingencies find that under higher load or more complex response control demands, children with ADHD show more response variability and less controlled choice behavior, again pointing to state-dependent degradation of decision quality.[^3_5]

Implication: eliciting long-term goals during or immediately after demanding tasks, multi-switch clinical interviews, or complex paperwork is likely to bias intentions toward short-term relief and reduce stability/consistency.

## Emotional arousal and “hot” vs “cool” states

- Delay-discounting literature in broader psychopathology suggests that mood and arousal states modulate temporal discounting; for example, depression studies show state-dependent changes in discounting of future rewards, with more negative mood linked to steeper discounting of large future rewards.[^3_6]
- The ADHD decision-making literature emphasizes suboptimal reward processing rather than pure risk seeking; tasks with immediately consumable rewards or more affectively engaging frames tend to amplify preference for immediate outcomes, consistent with dual-system (“hot vs cool”) accounts of intertemporal choice.[^3_7][^3_4]
- Neurocognitive work on ADHD as a timing disturbance links impulsiveness to abnormalities in temporal processing and dopaminergic systems; pharmacological normalization improves timing-related brain activity, which in turn is associated with more adaptive temporal decisions.[^3_8]

Implication: goals elicited in a “hot” state (frustrated, overwhelmed, highly activated) are likely to overweight immediate emotion regulation (“I just want to quit/stop everything”) and underweight long-term values; conversely, goals elicited in a low-arousal, dysphoric state may underweight future benefit and ambition.

## State-dependent preference reversals and intentions

- Intra-individual inconsistency in temporal decision making in ADHD indicates that the same individual may reverse preferences between immediate and delayed rewards across short time spans without systematic changes in objective contingencies.[^3_2][^3_1]
- The observed pattern—greater choice variability, context-sensitive shifts under cognitive load, and modulation by motivational conditions—supports the view that intention formation is highly state-dependent in ADHD: the “goal” at time 1 may not reflect the person’s behaviorally-relevant valuation at time 2.[^3_3][^3_1][^3_5]
- Studies linking ADHD symptoms, delay discounting, and procrastination in young adults suggest that state-dependent valuation (e.g., preferring immediate mood repair when under stress) contributes to intention–behavior gaps and shifting commitments over time.[^3_9][^3_4]

Implication: single-shot goal setting sessions risk capturing “local” preferences that are likely to reverse when state shifts (different day, emotional context, or cognitive load).

## Implications for when and how to elicit goals

Based on these findings, design choices that improve the quality and stability of elicited intentions in ADHD/executive dysfunction include:

1. **Choose low-load, “cool” windows for major goal decisions**
    - Schedule goal-setting when the person is not immediately post-task, fatigued, or in acute emotional distress, minimizing working memory load and arousal that bias toward immediate relief.[^3_8][^3_3][^3_5]
    - Keep the surrounding session cognitively simple when high-stakes, long-horizon goals are discussed; avoid embedding goal elicitation at the tail end of complex assessments or paperwork.[^3_3][^3_5]
2. **Use repeated sampling rather than one-off commitments**
    - Treat goal articulation as a hypothesis to be re-sampled: elicit the same goal areas across sessions or days and look for convergence rather than relying on a single expression.[^3_1][^3_2]
    - Brief “goal check” items (e.g., rating importance or preference for work vs health vs education goals) at multiple time points can reveal state-dependent swings; stable goals are those that survive across state changes.
3. **Make temporal structure explicit and concrete**
    - Because temporal processing in ADHD is noisy, support explicit discussion of near vs far consequences with visual timelines or concrete scenarios instead of abstract “six months from now” language.[^3_4][^3_8]
    - Anchor goals to specific, meaningful future events (course deadlines, contract renewal dates) rather than vague horizons, reducing the cognitive work of temporal translation.
4. **Segment “planning for later” from “deciding now”**
    - Use two-phase procedures: phase 1 identifies candidate long-term directions in a relatively neutral state; phase 2, possibly on a different day, confirms and narrows these into specific commitments after the person has “slept on it.”
    - When someone is in a hot state, record immediate preferences as data (“right now I strongly feel X”) but defer binding decisions or large goal changes until a calmer follow-up session.
5. **Design for robustness to state fluctuations**
    - Favor small, state-robust commitments (e.g., minimal viable actions that are acceptable in different moods and loads) over high-intensity plans that will be abandoned when state shifts.[^3_9][^3_4]
    - Build in explicit “if my future self feels differently” clauses (e.g., check-in points where goals can be renegotiated) to legitimize adaptation rather than covert reversal.

# Gaps and open problems in HCI and assistive technology design for intent

elicitation from neurodivergent users. Research agenda papers, critical
reviews, or failure analyses of existing approaches. NOT accessibility
compliance or WCAG audits. Focus on interaction design for goal and
preference communication. You can use community acknowledgements and not only academia

Intent elicitation for neurodivergent users is under-theorised in HCI: most systems assume stable, easily verbalised preferences and goals, whereas both research and community accounts point to fluctuating, hard-to-articulate intentions that are shaped by context, power, and cognitive load. Below are key gaps and open problems framed as a research agenda rather than WCAG-style accessibility work.[^4_1][^4_2][^4_3]

## 1. Under-specification of “intent” in neurodiversity-aligned HCI

- Neurodiversity papers in psychology and education argue for studying both strengths and difficulties and for centering lived experience, but they rarely operationalise how “preferences,” “goals,” or “needs” should be represented or elicited in interactive systems.[^4_2][^4_4][^4_3]
- Participatory translational science models for ADHD/autism emphasise involving neurodivergent people in setting research priorities, yet they still lean on conventional meetings, surveys, and workshops whose interactional formats may not support nuanced goal or preference communication for those with executive and communication differences.[^4_5][^4_3]

Open problem: develop explicit, testable models of *what* counts as intent (e.g., momentary desires, higher-order values, conditional preferences) that are compatible with fluctuating attention, demand sensitivity, and state-dependent decision making, and then embed those models into interface and protocol design.

## 2. Limited work on interaction patterns for joint meaning-making

- Recent HCI work with autistic adults on conversation technology used visual “swimlane” representations to help dyads construct and maintain common ground, showing that external visualisations can scaffold clarification and joint decision-making.[^4_1]
- This work highlights that standard turn-taking interviews and chat-based systems usually leave mental state inference implicit; the technology did better when it made *conversational structure* and contributions explicitly visible so participants could negotiate shared understanding.[^4_1]

Open problem: extend these kinds of visual, shared representations to *goal and preference elicitation*, not only social communication—e.g., shared canvases of candidate goals, uncertainty, and constraints that both user and system edit, instead of one-way form filling.

## 3. Gaps in tools for dynamic, state-dependent preferences

- Conceptual work on neurodiversity in education calls for contexts that adapt to fluctuating energy, processing capacity, and sensory load, but concrete interaction techniques for capturing *state-dependent* preferences (what I want/ can handle right now vs in general) are largely missing from assistive technology design.[^4_6][^4_4]
- Community discussions (e.g., autistic and ADHD forums and blogs) frequently mention that goal-setting and planning apps assume stable motivation and consistent executive capacity; users report “crash and burn” cycles where goals set in one state become oppressive or unmanageable in another, with little tooling to renegotiate intent without starting from scratch.[^4_3]

Open problem: design and evaluate mechanisms for *revisable*, multi-layered intent—e.g., separating durable values from situational goals, supporting “soft commitments,” and logging state when goals are set so systems can later flag likely state-related misalignment.

## 4. Insufficient alternatives to heavy verbal self-report

- Neurodiversity-aligned research calls for multimodal methods and explicitly warns against over-reliance on language-heavy, pathologising instruments, yet many HCI and assistive tools for planning still revolve around text boxes, structured questionnaires, and interviews.[^4_2][^4_3]
- Where visual methods are introduced (e.g., card sorts, timelines, pictorial tools in clinical and educational settings), they are often treated as auxiliary aids rather than a primary interaction paradigm for preference and goal articulation that could be systematically explored and refined.[^4_3][^4_1]

Open problem: treat alternative modalities—visual mapping, drag-and-drop prioritisation, tagging, example-based specification, conversational drawing—as *first-class* interaction techniques for intent elicitation, with empirical comparisons against verbal-only interfaces specifically in neurodivergent samples.

## 5. Limited critique and failure analysis of mainstream goal tools

- Across neurodiversity and compassionate pedagogy work, there is critique of environments that demand self-advocacy, self-regulation, and goal-setting without adjusting power dynamics and cognitive load, but there is little systematic HCI failure analysis of widely used tools such as learning management systems, PDP platforms, or coaching dashboards.[^4_4][^4_6][^4_3]
- Community accounts repeatedly describe mainstream planners and goal apps as “for people who already have executive function,” pointing to mismatches such as rigid recurring reminders, all-or-nothing completion tracking, and lack of support for ambivalence or partial success.[^4_3]

Open problem: conduct empirical, neurodivergent-led “bug reports” for goal-setting and planning tools—documenting specific interaction patterns that induce masking, shutdown, or demand avoidance and translating these into design anti-patterns and pattern libraries.

## 6. Underdeveloped participatory methods for intent elicitation

- Participatory research programmes in ADHD and autism emphasise co-production and shared governance but often still rely on conventional workshops and steering groups, which privilege fast, confident, verbally fluent contributors and can marginalise those with slower processing or anxiety about speaking.[^4_5][^4_3]
- The neurodiversity literature explicitly recommends that researchers recognise their own biases and listen to neurodivergent people, but gives limited practical guidance on *how* to structure participatory sessions, digital co-design tools, or asynchronous channels so that intent and preference expression is less constrained by executive and social pressures.[^4_2][^4_3]

Open problem: develop participatory HCI methods tuned to neurodivergent communication styles—e.g., longer asynchronous cycles; low-demand prompts; optional anonymity; multimodal submissions; and tools that allow participants to iteratively edit, retract, or reframe their stated goals and preferences.

## 7. Research agenda directions

Pulling these gaps together, a focused research agenda for HCI and assistive tech around intent elicitation with neurodivergent users could include:

- **Theory-building:** formalising models of layered, revisable intent (values, standing preferences, current-state preferences) consistent with neurodiversity perspectives.[^4_2][^4_3]
- **Design patterns:** cataloguing and experimentally evaluating interaction patterns for goal communication that externalise structure (visual canvases, timelines), lower verbal load, and support co-edited representations.[^4_1][^4_3]
- **State-aware interfaces:** building and testing systems that explicitly log and use cognitive/emotional state when goals are set, and that proactively invite renegotiation when context changes.
- **Failure case studies:** partnering with neurodivergent communities to systematically document how existing planning and coaching tools fail them, treating these accounts as central design evidence rather than anecdotal edge cases.[^4_5][^4_3]
- **Participatory infrastructure:** creating toolkits for neurodivergent-led co-design of intent-elicitation features, including templates for asynchronous workshops, consent to change one’s mind, and mechanisms to share power over which goals are even on the table.[^4_5][^4_3][^4_2]

# Demand avoidance and its effect on goal elicitation in autistic populations.

externally imposed frameworks triggers resistance or shutdown. Alternative
elicitation approaches tested in clinical practice or documented by
practitioners and neurodivergent-led communities. Peer-reviewed,

Demand avoidance can directly distort goal elicitation in autistic people: direct questions and structured objectives can be experienced as aversive demands, evoking resistance, negotiation, or shutdown rather than authentic preference expression. Evidence is still sparse and mostly indirect, but there are converging empirical, clinical, and community accounts with some documented alternative approaches.[^5_1][^5_2]

## How goal-focused demands can trigger avoidance

- “Extreme demand avoidance” (EDA/PDA) is characterised by obsessive resistance to everyday demands, need for control, and socially strategic avoidance (distraction, negotiation, withdrawal); it is strongly associated with autistic traits and anxiety.[^5_2][^5_1]
- The DISCO-based description of PDA includes marked avoidance of ordinary requests, sudden changes in behaviour when a task is framed as obligatory, and surface sociability masking poor social identity, implying that overt compliance in interviews may conceal covert resistance and later non-follow-through.[^5_2]
- Adult EDA research shows that higher demand avoidance correlates with higher anxiety and autistic traits; authors highlight that demands can be social (expectations, questions) as much as practical, and that even benign requests may be experienced as threats to autonomy.[^5_1]

Implications for elicitation:

- Direct “what are your goals?” or “we need to set three objectives” can be interpreted as non-negotiable demands, provoking immediate avoidance (topic change, humour, deferral), oppositional responses, or shutdown.
- Externally imposed frameworks (SMART grids, rigid performance targets) can be felt as loss of control; some autistic adults report willfully disengaging, giving superficial answers, or agreeing to goals they have no intention of pursuing to escape the interaction (described frequently in PDA community writing and blogs).[^5_1]


## Direct questioning and structured objective-setting

Empirical work is limited, but related strands point to problems with conventional, question-driven goal elicitation:

- Autistic adults frequently report that open, direct questions about internal states or preferences are hard to answer on the spot; this is linked to alexithymia, anxiety, and fear of “getting it wrong,” which can compound demand avoidance.[^5_3][^5_4]
- Clinical cognitive-behavioural and DBT adaptations for autistic people note that highly structured, therapist-driven homework and goal lists can trigger avoidance or apparent non-engagement; therapists describe needing to reduce explicit demands and co-create tasks to maintain participation.[^5_5][^5_6]
- Co-design of a goal-setting tool with autistic adolescents and adults found that standard service goal-setting approaches (interviews, forms) often failed to elicit meaningful goals; participants experienced them as overwhelming and misaligned with what they actually cared about and could manage.[^5_7]

Implications:

- “Top-down” performance management style goal interviews (especially in schools or services) risk capturing defensive, minimal, or socially desirable goals rather than genuine aims.
- Repeated re-framing of questions (“but what do you *really* want?”) can escalate pressure and anxiety, increasing avoidance rather than insight.


## Alternative low-demand / autonomy-supportive approaches

Across clinical practice, co-design, and community writing, several specific alternatives recur.

### 1. Collaborative, visual goal tools

- The co-designed autistic goal-setting tool used pictorial domain cards (e.g., sleep, sensory environment, friendships), sorting, and prioritisation, then collaboratively turned selections into goals; this reduced the need for on-the-spot verbal generation and made the process feel more shared and less interrogative.[^5_7]
- Practitioners reported that giving people tangible options to move around, rank, or discard helped circumvent immediate “no” reactions to abstract questions and allowed indirect signalling of priorities (e.g., repeatedly moving certain cards higher).[^5_7]

Key features:

- Choice from visible options rather than direct demand for self-generated statements.
- Ability to *not* choose certain domains without justification, which preserves a sense of control.


### 2. Graded, indirect, or “sideways” questions

- Autistic-led and PDA-focused parenting and practitioner communities advocate “low-demand” or “gentle parenting” strategies: offering choices, using suggestions rather than commands, and embedding tasks into mutually agreed routines rather than issuing direct instructions.[^5_1]
- Translated to goal elicitation, this includes asking about “what feels hardest / easiest right now,” “what you’d like to change about a typical day,” or “things you’d like more/less of,” rather than “state your goals.” These are extensively described in PDA handbooks, blogs, and training materials, even if not yet formalised in trials.[^5_1]

Key features:

- Emphasis on curiosity and exploration, not evaluation.
- Framing goals as experiments or preferences, not obligations.


### 3. Sharing control over process and pacing

- Neurodiversity-informed and participatory autism research emphasises shared agenda-setting and flexible participation—allowing people to choose topics, formats (text, drawing, asynchronous), and timing of contributions.[^5_4][^5_8][^5_3]
- Clinical adaptations of psychotherapies for autistic people often reduce sessional “homework” demands and allow clients to opt in to specific tasks; therapists note that autonomy and collaboration are essential for engagement.[^5_6][^5_9][^5_5]

Applied to goal elicitation:

- Allow the person to decide *when* goal discussions happen in a meeting, whether they want advance notice or pre-written prompts, and whether they’d prefer asynchronous reflection (e.g., writing or messaging later).
- Normalize “I don’t know yet” and treat goals as drafts that can be edited without penalty, reducing the stakes of committing.


### 4. Asynchronous and written/self-paced methods

- Autistic community accounts often describe online, asynchronous communication as lower-demand and more controllable than real-time conversation; this is reflected in calls for more email- or chat-based adjustments in education and services.[^5_10][^5_11][^5_4]
- Some autistic-led initiatives recommend providing planning prompts in advance and collecting responses via forms, shared documents, or message threads that can be edited over time, rather than in-situ questioning; users report this reduces pressure and allows more accurate reflection.[^5_4]

Key features:

- Time to process and revise, which can help bypass immediate oppositional reactions to being put on the spot.
- Ability to step away and come back when overwhelmed.


### 5. Value- and interest-first framing

- Autistic advocates frequently report that goal frameworks that ignore special interests or sensory needs feel alien and demand-heavy; in contrast, interest-based interventions and strengths-focused planning are experienced as more intrinsically motivating and less coercive.[^5_3][^5_4]
- Clinical and educational practice papers on autism increasingly argue for starting from personal interests and values, using these as anchors for any change targets, rather than imposing generic social or independence goals.[^5_9][^5_6][^5_3]

Applied to elicitation:

- Begin with “what you enjoy / what matters to you,” and jointly explore small changes that would protect or expand those, rather than leading with deficits or service metrics.
- Use goals that explicitly include protecting autonomy or reducing unwanted demands (e.g., “reduce situations where you feel trapped”) as legitimate outcomes, not “non-compliance.”


## Design implications for your own methods

From this evidence and community practice, methods that are less likely to trigger demand avoidance when eliciting goals from autistic people typically:

- **Minimise direct, high-stakes questions**: replace “we need to decide your goals” with exploratory prompts, visual tools, and iterative conversations.
- **Offer genuine choice and opt-out**: including the ability to decline certain domains, delay decisions, or frame goals as “experiments” rather than fixed obligations.
- **Externalise control and structure**: shared visual boards, card sorts, and written drafts that both parties can adjust, so the autistic person is not just responding to demands but actively shaping the representation.
- **Use asynchronous, multi-modal channels**: especially for refining or confirming goals, to avoid on-the-spot pressure and allow rethinking without confrontation.
