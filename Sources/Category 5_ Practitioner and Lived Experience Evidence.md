# ADHD coaching methods for goal clarification and priority articulation.

Structured techniques used by certified ADHD coaches for helping clients
identify what they want, decompose objectives, and sequence actions.
Practitioner-published methods, training materials, or documented
protocols. NOT generic life coaching or motivational content.

Certified ADHD coaches typically use structured, scripted processes for helping clients clarify goals, define priorities, and break work into sequenced actions, often codified in ADHD‑specific competencies, training manuals, and program protocols.[^1_1][^1_2][^1_3][^1_4]

## ADHD‑specific coaching competencies

Professional bodies such as PAAC (Professional Association for ADHD Coaches) define ADHD coaching as a collaborative, goal‑oriented process in which coach and client identify the client’s goals and develop self‑awareness, systems, skills, and strategies to achieve them. PAAC’s core competencies emphasise partnering with the client to identify what they want to accomplish in a given session, define measures of success, explore why the goal matters, and clarify what needs to be addressed to achieve it.[^1_2][^1_4][^1_1]

Within these competencies, coaches are expected to co‑create explicit session agendas, reconfirm goals, and tie actions to the client’s values and meaning, rather than simply asking for generic goals. ADHD‑specific competencies also stress managing overwhelm by chunking and prioritising tasks, reinforcing strengths, and using external structures and prompts to support follow‑through.[^1_4][^1_1]

## Structured goal‑clarification techniques

ADHD coaching training materials describe a set of repeatable micro‑protocols for clarifying goals at both engagement and session level. A typical session‑opening sequence includes: (1) asking what the client wants to accomplish today, (2) co‑defining concrete success measures, (3) exploring what makes this important, and (4) identifying what the client believes needs to be addressed to reach that outcome. Practitioner accounts also emphasise exploring the gap between current and desired states, eliciting assumptions, and refining an initially broad “fix everything” wish into a narrower, experience‑based goal (for example, shifting from “fix my job” to “have a better workday by prioritising classroom experience”).[^1_5][^1_6][^1_3][^1_4]

Prevatt and Levrini’s clinical ADHD coaching model includes structured tools explicitly for goal clarification, such as the ADHD Life Wheel (mapping satisfaction across domains to surface priority areas), a Decision‑Making Table (laying out options, pros/cons, and required actions), and a Processing Exercise that helps clients articulate what they actually want from a given situation. These tools function as visual, stepwise scaffolds that make abstract goals concrete and reduce decision overload for clients with ADHD.[^1_3]

## Decomposing objectives and sequencing actions

ADHD‑specific training booklets lay out explicit criteria for moving from clarified goals to plans, including “reducing ADHD task overwhelm by identifying successful, individual steps” and “establishing priorities, next steps and timeline.” Coaches are instructed to co‑design customised structures and systems, employ assistive technologies, and create external memory prompts to support stepwise implementation. Practitioner programmes also integrate evidence‑based behaviour‑change frameworks (for example, the Transtheoretical Model) to match action planning to readiness for change, and to define small behavioural steps consistent with the client’s current stage.[^1_7][^1_4]

Applied ADHD coaching texts describe session phases covering “nuts and bolts” implementation: after goal clarification and suitability assessment, coaches guide clients through breaking goals into sub‑tasks, sequencing these, assigning due dates, and using tools such as the Eisenhower Matrix for priority sorting. Training materials additionally reference “winning strategies” such as converting vague intentions into concrete actions (“good–better–best” or graded plans), using body doubling for initiation, and explicitly planning transitions and follow‑through.[^1_8][^1_3][^1_4]

## Documented protocols and training resources

Several practitioner‑facing resources provide semi‑formal protocols rather than generic life‑coaching advice. For example, a CHADD coaching article for adults with ADHD outlines evidence‑based coaching elements such as defining small, behaviourally specific steps, setting realistic expectations, and building external supports and accountability. A CHADD “Attention” article on coaching students with ADHD recommends creating a menu of concrete, reachable goals, co‑generating reinforcers, and teaching goal‑setting explicitly rather than assuming students can self‑define objectives.[^1_9][^1_3][^1_7][^1_4]

Comprehensive ADHD coaching guides for mental health professionals present step‑by‑step protocols for intake, initial sessions, middle‑phase coaching, and termination, with forms, rating scales, and named exercises for goal clarification, prioritisation, and planning. Contemporary ADHD coaching training modules for working with adults with ADHD codify these elements into competency‑based checklists that trainers use to assess whether coaches are adequately clarifying session goals, defining measures of success, scaffolding priority decisions, and supporting clients in designing concrete, sequenced action plans.[^1_3][^1_4]

# Neurodivergent-developed frameworks for self-understanding, intention

clarification, and executive function support. Methods created by or
with neurodivergent communities. Documented approaches from autistic
and ADHD self-advocacy, peer support, or community practice. Prioritize
methods with described steps or structure over personal narrative.

Several neurodivergent communities have developed structured frameworks and tools for self-understanding, intention clarification, and executive function support, often combining identity work with practical scaffolding.[^2_1][^2_2][^2_3][^2_4]

## Autistic-led self-understanding frameworks

Autistic-led work on self-determination frames self-understanding as identifying personal values, needs, and environmental conditions that enable “being able to be myself.” Qualitative studies with autistic adults describe three recurring axes: opportunities to make choices and exert autonomy, support to unmask and be valued as one’s authentic autistic self, and pragmatic support for executive processing differences. These studies translate into practice recommendations to map situations where autonomy is constrained, articulate preferred ways of doing things (sensory, social, pacing), and then design supports that preserve autonomy while reducing cognitive load.[^2_5][^2_1]

The SPELL framework (developed by the National Autistic Society) is widely used in UK autistic practice and training to structure understanding and support. SPELL stands for Structure, Positive approaches and expectations, Empathy, Low arousal, and Links, and is intended as a flexible, person‑centred framework for profiling an individual’s needs, preferences, and stressors, then designing environmental and relational supports around them. Training materials contain stepwise exercises (for example, mapping morning routines, identifying stress points, and then adding structure or visual supports) that help autistic people and supporters collaboratively analyse daily demands and adjust them.[^2_2][^2_6][^2_7]

## Autistic traits and self-assessment tools

The Self‑Assessment of Autistic Traits (SAAT) was developed under the leadership of autistic researchers using first‑person descriptions and iterative feedback from a panel of autistic experts. It was designed to centre inner autistic experience in assessment, moving beyond clinician‑created checklists. The development paper describes structured stages: collecting self‑descriptions, generating candidate items, refining through autistic expert review, and psychometric evaluation. In practice, the SAAT functions as a semi‑structured self‑reflection tool that helps autistic adults identify patterns in sensory, social, cognitive, and emotional experience, which can then inform intention setting and support planning.[^2_8]

Autistic‑oriented profiling tools such as the Autism Progress framework use levelled descriptors of communication, social interaction, and flexibility to document how a person expresses preferences, initiates actions, and signals needs. These descriptors are structured developmentally (for example, levels at which individuals begin to communicate consistent preferences or indicate needs and intentions), and can be used to scaffold self‑advocacy and planning conversations at an appropriate level of abstraction.[^2_9]

## Executive function support from neurodivergent practitioners

Neurodivergent‑run platforms such as Neurodivergent Insights provide structured “executive functioning helpers” that explicitly target ADHD and autistic profiles. Their Executive Functioning Workbook, co‑created by an ADHD coach and psychologist, is organised into chapters on task initiation, working memory, organisation, time management, and planning, each with psychoeducation, menus of strategies, and space to record experiments. They also recommend neurodivergent‑developed digital tools like Goblin Tools’ “Magic ToDo,” which automatically breaks a large, vague task into smaller steps to reduce planning load.[^2_4]

Community‑facing resources from autistic and ADHD charities and training collaborations emphasise externalising executive processes: using visual schedules, routines, clear stepwise tasks, and predictable structures to support planning and initiation. Implementation guidance stresses structuring tasks with clear steps, using visual prompts, and aligning demands with the person’s interests and energy patterns, which collectively function as an environmentally‑anchored executive function scaffold.[^2_7][^2_10][^2_2]

## Neurodivergent-created goal and intention methods

Some autistic/ADHD creators explicitly adapt evidence‑based goal methods (for example, mental contrasting plus implementation intentions, “MC2”) for neurodivergent brains. In one AudHD‑led teaching resource, mental contrasting is used to connect desired outcomes with current obstacles, and “if‑then” implementation intentions are used to outsource initiation to situational cues, reducing reliance on working memory and internal motivation. The method is presented stepwise: (1) vividly imagine the desired outcome, (2) identify the most relevant obstacle, (3) formulate concrete if‑then plans linking cues to micro‑actions, and (4) test and adjust.[^2_3]

ADHD‑specific intention‑setting resources from neurodivergent coaches describe multi‑step processes focused on emotional connection and future‑self imagery rather than abstract goals. For example, a five‑step intention protocol might include clarifying how one wants to feel, connecting with a future self, identifying a small aligned action, preparing to lean into discomfort, and then focusing on the anticipated end‑state feeling. These methods are explicitly framed as compensatory strategies for “brains of interest, not importance,” making intention emotionally salient enough to drive initiation.[^2_11]

## Community self-advocacy and autonomy frameworks

Autistic self‑advocacy work on self‑advocacy and autonomy proposes structured frameworks for building skills such as articulating needs, understanding rights, and planning disclosures. Tutorials for professionals emphasise co‑developed self‑advocacy programmes grounded in the social model of disability, with explicit steps: exploring identity and narrative, mapping barriers, teaching specific advocacy skills (for example, making requests, negotiation), and practising in real contexts. Participatory studies on autonomy and self‑determination with autistic adults with and without intellectual disability use structured group sessions (for example, weekly discussions, arts and games) to help participants articulate what autonomy means to them and how they want to be supported.[^2_12][^2_1][^2_5]

Newer work on “ethical listening” frameworks for non‑speaking autistic participants also offers structured processes for recording and interpreting non‑verbal communication as a form of self‑expression. This framework recommends specific tools—video, behaviour checklists, and autism‑affirming theories—and outlines stages from initial engagement to data analysis, with the goal of capturing authentic autistic perspectives in decision‑making.[^2_13]

# Occupational therapy approaches to goal elicitation and priority-setting

for adults with executive dysfunction. Clinical protocols, assessment
tools, and practitioner-developed methods. Canadian Occupational
Performance Measure and comparable instruments. How occupational
therapists structure the goal-identification conversation. Practitioner
and peer-reviewed literature.

Occupational therapists use structured, client-centred interviewing and strategy-training approaches (notably the COPM and CO-OP) to elicit personally meaningful goals, prioritise them, and link them to executive function interventions in adults with executive dysfunction.[^3_1][^3_2][^3_3][^3_4]

## Canadian Occupational Performance Measure (COPM)

The COPM is a semi‑structured interview used to identify, prioritise, and rate occupational performance problems in self‑care, productivity, and leisure. Adults are invited to describe difficulties in everyday activities; the therapist then helps them generate a list of problems and rate each item’s importance on a 1–10 scale. The client selects up to five of the most important problems as goals, and rates current performance and satisfaction for each on 1–10 scales, providing baseline data for later comparison. Studies in geriatric and community rehabilitation describe the COPM as shifting practice toward occupation‑based, client‑centred, goal‑directed rehabilitation and increasing patients’ involvement in treatment planning.[^3_5][^3_3][^3_6][^3_7][^3_4][^3_8]

Qualitative work with therapists highlights how the COPM structures the conversation: starting with open exploration of “a day in the life,” moving to clarification of specific activities, then to importance ratings and collaborative selection of priorities. Therapists report using probing questions (for example, “What matters most for you to be able to do at home?”) and reflective summaries to help clients with cognitive impairments articulate and focus goals.[^3_9][^3_6][^3_10][^3_5]

## CO-OP Approach: Goal–Plan–Do–Check

The Cognitive Orientation to daily Occupational Performance (CO‑OP) is a task‑oriented, meta‑cognitive intervention widely applied with adults and older adults who have executive dysfunction after acquired brain injury, stroke, or hip fracture. CO‑OP begins with the client identifying several personally meaningful everyday goals, typically using the COPM to elicit and operationalise them. Therapy sessions then use the global meta‑cognitive strategy “Goal–Plan–Do–Check” to guide the person’s problem-solving: defining the specific task goal, generating a plan, carrying it out, and then checking what worked or not.[^3_2][^3_11][^3_12][^3_13][^3_8][^3_1]

RCTs and pilot studies with adults with executive dysfunction show that CO‑OP, compared to usual occupational therapy, yields greater improvement on self‑identified goals and sometimes on executive function measures, with transfer to untrained tasks. In practice, therapists structure each session around one or more goals chosen from the client’s COPM list, support the client to analyse performance breakdowns (for example, planning, sequencing, monitoring), co‑create strategies, and explicitly reflect on performance to build generalisable problem‑solving skills.[^3_11][^3_14][^3_13][^3_8][^3_1][^3_2]

## Occupational Goal Intervention (OGI) method

The Occupational Goal Intervention (OGI) method is a manualised OT protocol designed to improve executive function in people with severe mental illness (treatment‑resistant schizophrenia) by organising rehabilitation around personally meaningful occupational goals. In an RCT, participants received 30 sessions over 15 weeks, with the OGI group showing medium to large gains in executive function and everyday functioning compared with a craft‑activity control. The OGI protocol starts with structured interviews to identify client‑valued occupational goals, uses goal attainment scaling, and then trains planning, problem‑solving, and monitoring within these real‑life activities.[^3_15]

Although developed in psychiatry, OGI illustrates an OT pattern: use of goal‑attainment methods and occupation‑based practice to embed executive‑function training into day‑to‑day tasks. The method emphasises breaking goals into sub‑tasks, scheduling and environmental structuring, and repeated practice with feedback, all anchored to the client’s chosen goals.[^3_15]

## OT goal‑setting and priority‑setting conversations

Across cognitive rehabilitation, OT practice guidelines describe goal‑setting conversations that explicitly focus on everyday tasks requiring attention, memory, planning and sequencing, and prioritise those that matter most to the individual. In cognitive rehabilitation for early dementia, for example, OT‑delivered goal‑setting involves collaboratively identifying specific daily tasks, agreeing on clear functional targets, and then practising compensatory strategies (such as checklists, external cues, or environmental modifications) tied to those goals. AOTA guidance on functional cognition underscores using performance of real‑world tasks (for example, bathing, meal preparation, medication management) as the starting point for identifying cognitive breakdowns and generating function‑oriented goals.[^3_16][^3_17][^3_18]

In community rehabilitation services where the COPM is used routinely, chart reviews show that therapists consistently record clients’ stated problems, importance ratings, selected priority goals, and follow‑up performance/satisfaction scores, indicating a structured, reproducible process. Delphi work with expert OTs notes that training is needed to conduct this interview effectively, particularly for people with cognitive impairments, and that therapists adapt pacing, cueing, and use of prompts to scaffold goal articulation.[^3_10][^3_4]

## Assessment tools and methods that feed into goal setting

OTs often combine the COPM with other tools to characterise executive dysfunction and support goal negotiation. Performance‑based executive function tests (for example, the Behavioural Assessment of the Dysexecutive Syndrome) and functional assessments (for example, Direct Assessment of Functional Status) are used in research to quantify change but are complemented clinically by observation, checklists, and structured interviews. OT‑oriented resources on executive function testing emphasise triangulating information from standardised tests, rating scales, contextual checklists, observation, and client/family interviews to build an executive function profile linked directly to school, work, or home occupations.[^3_19][^3_20][^3_21][^3_15]

In practice, occupational therapists then use this information to refine and prioritise goals—for example, focusing on time management for medication, organisation for financial management, or planning for meal preparation—rather than on abstract cognitive test scores. This keeps goal setting anchored in functional outcomes while still targeting specific executive processes through environmental adaptations, compensatory strategies, and meta‑cognitive training.[^3_17][^3_18][^3_1][^3_2][^3_16]
