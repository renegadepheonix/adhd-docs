# Executive function deficits in ADHD and goal formulation. Empirical evidence

on how working memory, cognitive flexibility, and inhibition impair the ability
to identify, sequence, and communicate intentions. Peer-reviewed or
practitioner-published. Cognitive psychology, clinical neuroscience, and
neuropsychological rehabilitation literature. NOT self-management advice
or productivity content.

Executive function deficits in ADHD impair the internal representation of goals, the structuring of action sequences, and the translation of intentions into verbally communicated plans, largely via disruptions in working memory, cognitive flexibility, and inhibition. Below is a synthesis anchored in cognitive/clinical/neuropsychological literature, focusing on intention formation and goal-directed behavior rather than self-help content.[^1_1][^1_2][^1_3][^1_4][^1_5]

## Theoretical accounts linking EF to intentions in ADHD

Barkley’s behavioral inhibition model conceptualizes ADHD as a primary deficit in behavioral **inhibition**, which then compromises four EF-dependent processes: working memory, self-regulation of affect–motivation–arousal, internalization of speech, and reconstitution (analysis/synthesis of behavior). These processes are proposed to underlie self-directed goal formulation, the maintenance of intentions over time, and the organization of behavior into coherent sequences. Empirical reviews supporting the model report robust deficits in behavioral inhibition and working memory, with consequent impairments in self-directed rule use and forward planning in ADHD.[^1_2][^1_6]

Barkley’s later updated executive functioning model elevates working memory from a secondary consequence of inhibition deficits to a primary, partially independent driver of ADHD symptoms, alongside inhibition. This reframing explicitly positions working memory and inhibition as joint determinants of goal-directed action, including the capacity to hold intentions in mind and manipulate them in service of plans.[^1_7]

Neuroanatomically informed accounts emphasize frontostriatal and frontoparietal circuitry supporting these EF processes and propose that early inhibitory deficits “set the stage” developmentally for later-emerging working memory and higher-order planning difficulties, which together compromise complex goal management. These models align with the observation that more complex prospective memory and planning deficits become evident particularly from late childhood into adulthood.[^1_3][^1_6][^1_5]

## Working memory and goal/plan representation

Working memory deficits in ADHD are strongly implicated in failures to maintain, update, and manipulate intentions and multi-step plans. A clinical review focused on working memory and organizational skills in ADHD argues that impaired working memory produces broad, multisetting organizational impairment, making it “extraordinarily difficult” to consistently anticipate, plan, enact, and maintain goal-directed actions. The authors link these deficits to reduced capacity to hold task goals and relevant information online, to monitor progress, and to adjust actions in real time.[^1_8][^1_9][^1_4][^1_1]

A study explicitly examining elements of working memory as predictors of goal-setting skills in children with ADHD found that children with ADHD showed poorer performance than controls on central executive tasks and on the mastery components of goal-setting measures, despite similar strategy scores. Regression analyses indicated that central executive functions were critical predictors of variance in goal-setting skills, implicating these working memory-driven processes in the ability to generate and structure goals, rather than merely select strategies.[^1_4]

Recent experimental work that manipulated working memory and inhibitory demands in children with ADHD shows that increasing working memory load slows responses and reduces accuracy on inhibition tasks, with larger detrimental effects for children with ADHD than for clinical comparison groups. This supports the claim that when working memory resources are taxed, the system’s ability to maintain task goals and suppress irrelevant responses collapses, directly disrupting the execution of intended action sequences.[^1_9][^1_8]

In adult populations, studies of complex prospective memory (see below) also implicate working memory in constructing and retaining multi-step plans, particularly when tasks require juggling several delayed intentions. Across these lines of evidence, working memory emerges as the primary mechanism for representing the “mental script” of what one intends to do and in what order, with ADHD-associated deficits degrading that representational scaffold.[^1_1][^1_3][^1_4]

## Inhibition and intention formation, sequencing, and protection

Inhibition in ADHD is central both theoretically and empirically to intention-related problems. Barkley’s model treats behavioral inhibition (stopping prepotent responses, stopping ongoing responses, and interference control) as the foundational deficit that undermines the other EF processes needed for self-directed, goal-governed behavior. Reduced inhibition compromises the ability to suppress dominant or immediately salient responses long enough to consider distal goals, generate alternative actions, and select a coherent plan.[^1_6][^1_9][^1_2]

The complex prospective memory study in adults with ADHD found a large impairment specifically in task planning, with only negligible to small differences in plan recall, self-initiation, and execution. Inhibition was identified as a significant contributor to performance on task planning, suggesting that the ability to inhibit immediate impulses is directly involved in constructing coherent plans for future actions. This pattern indicates that adults with ADHD may especially struggle at the initial stage of transforming intentions into structured plans, rather than merely forgetting to act later.[^1_3]

In children, work on prospective memory stages indicates that ADHD is associated with impulsive planning when forming delayed intentions, and that this impulsivity at the formulation stage has downstream consequences for retention and implementation of those intentions. Children with ADHD showed comparable switching between ongoing tasks but made more performance errors during intention execution, suggesting that inadequate inhibitory control at encoding leaves intentions poorly specified and vulnerable to interference.[^1_5]

Experimental evidence that taxing working memory worsens inhibition performance disproportionately in children with ADHD further illustrates that inhibition and working memory jointly support the protection of intentions from distraction and competing drives. When working memory resources are insufficient to maintain the goal, inhibitory control cannot effectively prioritize goal-consistent responses over immediate, conflicting tendencies, undermining both sequencing and completion of intended actions.[^1_8][^1_9]

## Cognitive flexibility, plan restructuring, and communication of intentions

Cognitive flexibility—set shifting, switching between rules, and updating task sets—underpins the ability to adjust goals and action sequences as conditions change, to refine ill-formed intentions, and to translate them into communicable plans. Although the ADHD literature has historically focused more on inhibition and working memory, more recent work highlights flexibility as a key determinant of higher-order executive functions such as planning, problem-solving, and fluid intelligence.[^1_10][^1_11][^1_12]

Evidence from non-ADHD samples shows that cognitive flexibility predicts performance on planning and fluid intelligence tasks, underscoring its role in organizing complex, multi-step behavior. In neurodevelopmental groups with executive deficits (including ADHD), a tablet-based cognitive flexibility intervention showed that targeted flexibility training can improve flexibility and general goal-directed behavior, especially in tasks requiring shifting between operations, suggesting a causal relationship. Meta-analytic work on exercise-based interventions in ADHD similarly finds that both closed- and open-skill exercise can significantly improve cognitive flexibility, working memory, and inhibitory control, with associated improvements in executive functioning behavior.[^1_11][^1_12][^1_10]

In ADHD, the prospective memory paradigm in children found that although multitask switching per se was comparable to controls, this came at the expense of intention execution, with more performance errors. This pattern implies that flexibility may be applied reactively to manage immediate switching demands but is not effectively harnessed to maintain the structure of intentions across tasks. From a cognitive-communication standpoint, difficulties in flexibility can impede the capacity to reorganize and verbalize intentions when plans change, leaving individuals with ADHD either perseverating on outdated intentions or failing to articulate revised goals.[^1_5]

More broadly, executive functions, particularly working memory and cognitive flexibility, have been shown to explain unique variance in expressive language abilities, beyond age and nonverbal intelligence, in typically developing children. Although this work is not ADHD-specific, it suggests a mechanistic route by which EF deficits could disrupt the ability to verbally formulate and communicate intentions: limited verbal working memory and flexibility constrain the complexity and coherence of language used to describe planned actions.[^1_13]

## Prospective memory, goal management, and intention failures in ADHD

Prospective memory (PM)—remembering to carry out intended actions in the future—provides a natural framework for dissecting how EF deficits in ADHD affect each stage from intention formation to execution. The four main stages are: forming the intention, retaining it, self-initiating at the appropriate moment, and executing the intended action.[^1_3][^1_5]

In children with ADHD, experimental work using a computer multitask paradigm indicates that they may show particular difficulty forming delayed intentions, as evidenced by impulsive planning during the intention formation stage. This impulsive planning is associated with further problems in retaining and implementing intentions, and with increased performance errors during execution, despite broadly preserved task-switching ability. The findings align with the view that EF deficits in ADHD primarily compromise the initial structuring and protection of intentions, rather than only the triggering of actions at the right time.[^1_5]

In adults with ADHD, a complex prospective memory study similarly found that impairments were most pronounced in task planning, with only small effects on plan recall, self-initiation, and execution. The study identified four cognitive components contributing to PM performance and concluded that the primary PM impairment in adults with ADHD arises from deficient planning. Inhibition again emerged as an important contributor to planning performance, linking inhibitory control directly to the ability to organize future actions into a coherent sequence.[^1_3]

These PM data dovetail with rehabilitation frameworks such as Goal Management Training (GMT) developed in acquired brain injury, which explicitly target the processes of goal identification, initiation, monitoring, and maintenance. GMT and related interventions conceptualize failures at any of these stages as manifestations of EF breakdown, particularly in sustained attention, working memory, and plan monitoring, and they have been shown to remediate attentional and goal-management deficits in brain injury populations. While not ADHD-specific, these models are directly transferable for understanding how EF deficits prevent individuals from turning intentions into coordinated action sequences and from maintaining a coherent representation of goals over time.[^1_14]

Across ADHD studies, broader work on organizational skills and EF behaviors reinforces this picture: children and adults with ADHD show pervasive difficulties with anticipating tasks, organizing materials, tracking deadlines, and sustaining stepwise progress, with working memory deficits implicated as a core driver and inhibition and flexibility shaping how intentions are sequenced, protected, and restructured in context.[^1_15][^1_16][^1_1]

# Preference elicitation and priority articulation in autistic adults. Empirical

studies on barriers to identifying, ranking, and communicating objectives.
Adapted clinical or occupational therapy methods. Systematic reviews
preferred. Exclude pediatric populations. Include social communication
research where it directly addresses preference or intent expression.

Autistic adults describe substantial barriers to identifying, prioritizing, and communicating what matters to them, and emerging work in self‑determination, mental health, and OT has begun to develop adapted tools and practices for preference elicitation and goal setting. The literature is still fragmentary and leans heavily qualitative and participatory, with very few formal systematic reviews that focus narrowly on preference/priority articulation in adults.[^2_1][^2_2][^2_3][^2_4]

## Barriers to identifying and prioritizing goals

Qualitative work on self‑determination in autistic adults without intellectual disability shows that participants experience both external and internal barriers to making and articulating choices. Externally, they report ableist expectations, constrained options, and service structures that presume limited decision‑making competence, resulting in “limited options” and decisions pre‑shaped by others. Internally, participants consistently describe “executive processing differences” (planning, initiation, flexible thinking, emotional regulation) that make weighing options, comparing outcomes, and maintaining a stable sense of “what I want” effortful and sometimes overwhelming.[^2_2][^2_3]

These studies frame preference identification itself as a cognitively demanding, executive‑function‑laden task, not just a communication issue. Participants report doubting their capacity to make “appropriate” choices due to long histories of having their judgment questioned, which further undermines confidence in expressing priorities. Together, these findings suggest a dynamic interaction: external invalidation limits opportunities to practice autonomous choice, while executive and emotional regulation differences make complex priority ranking more resource‑intensive in real‑world contexts.[^2_3][^2_2]

## Barriers to communicating preferences and intentions

Critical‑incident and psychotherapy studies with autistic adults highlight communication‑related barriers that directly impact preference and intent expression in clinical encounters. Autistic clients describe difficulties when therapists use vague or indirect questions about “goals” or “feelings,” expect rapid in‑session responses, or fail to provide concrete options or written materials to support processing. These conditions can make it harder to formulate and verbalize what they want from therapy, leading to misaligned goals and a sense of not being heard.[^2_5]

Real‑world communication‑monitoring research shows that autistic adults tend to preferentially use written over verbal channels (emails, messaging apps) compared with neurotypical controls, while being equally socially engaged overall. This pattern is interpreted as written communication providing greater control over timing, reduced social anxiety, and better conditions for organizing and expressing thoughts. From a preference‑elicitation standpoint, this suggests that spoken, time‑pressured conversation may systematically under‑sample what autistic adults would articulate if allowed to use asynchronous, text‑based formats.[^2_6]

More broadly, qualitative work on autonomy and self‑determination emphasizes that choice and control are experienced not only as “being allowed to choose,” but as having one’s preferences interpreted accurately and respected, including the preference to decline options. Participants stress that not choosing an offered option is itself a meaningful preference signal, but is often pathologized or overridden in services. These dynamics can distort observed priorities, particularly in structured service planning where non‑endorsement may be treated as indecision rather than a principled choice.[^2_1]

## Self‑determination, executive processes, and goal‑directed action

A conceptual paper integrating self‑determination theory with executive‑process research in autistic adolescents and young adults argues that autonomy and goal‑directed action depend on both opportunity structures and individual executive capacities. The authors propose that executive processes (working memory, planning, cognitive flexibility) mediate the translation of personal motives into self‑determined, goal‑directed behavior, and that “executive processing differences” in autism alter how goals are generated, sequenced, and pursued in everyday life. They recommend that assessment and intervention explicitly target self‑determination skills (e.g., choice‑making, problem‑solving, self‑advocacy) alongside executive supports rather than focusing narrowly on symptom reduction.[^2_7]

Complementary qualitative work on autonomy in autistic adults underscores that executive‑processing differences (organization, initiation, shifting, emotion regulation) are experienced as direct barriers to being self‑determined in daily life, particularly when making complex or multi‑step decisions. Participants describe needing more time, structure, and environmental scaffolding to compare options and predict downstream consequences, which in turn shapes how they articulate priorities in clinical, educational, or support settings.[^2_3]

## Adapted tools and methods for eliciting goals and priorities

### Co‑designed goal‑setting tools

A participatory study co‑designed and iteratively refined the Adolescent and Adult Goal Setting Tool (AAGST) with autistic people and their families, explicitly in response to autistic reports that existing tools do not adequately support them to identify and explain goals that are important to them. The tool uses goal “cards” covering a broad range of life domains, visual supports, and structured prompts to help users explore, select, and communicate personally meaningful goals. The design emphasized accessible language, flexibility to accommodate diverse autistic profiles, and formats that reduce expressive load while still allowing detailed elaboration when desired.[^2_4]

Although the initial paper is developmental rather than outcome‑evaluative, it documents concrete design principles for preference‑elicitation tools: multiple representation formats (text, visuals), broad domain coverage to trigger recognition of latent priorities, and co‑production to ensure that categories reflect autistic adults’ lived concerns rather than clinician assumptions. These principles are directly applicable to clinical and occupational therapy contexts where autistic adults are asked to set rehabilitation or support goals.[^2_4]

### Neurodiversity‑affirming and priorities‑aligned practice guidance

Occupational therapy and social‑work guidance documents for autistic adults, while not always empirical, synthesize available evidence and community input into practice principles that center preference and priority articulation. OT practice guidelines emphasize self‑determination, autonomy, and collaboration, recommending that practitioners use explicit, client‑led goal setting and attend to autistic communication preferences (e.g., written communication, extra processing time, visual supports) when eliciting goals.[^2_8][^2_9][^2_10]

The UK Capabilities Statement for social work with autistic adults, based on autistic input, explicitly instructs practitioners to begin encounters with open but concrete prompts such as “what are your priorities today?” to allow autistic adults to set the agenda. It also stresses supporting autistic adults to “identify their life choices (and preferences) and to make decisions about all aspects of their lives,” recognizing that opportunities to articulate preferences are often constrained by service structures and gatekeeping. These recommendations implicitly treat priority articulation as a skill that can be scaffolded through environment, question design, and respect for alternative communication modes.[^2_10]

A neurodiversity‑affirming OT discussion paper on daily living skills similarly argues that therapists should focus on supporting autistic people to do “everyday things” in ways that align with their identity and sensory and social preferences, rather than trying to normalize behavior. While not a formal review, it frames goal setting as a process of jointly identifying valued activities and acceptable forms of participation, and highlights the need to avoid overriding autistic preferences in the name of independence or normative functioning.[^2_11]

### Clinical encounters and psychotherapy adaptations

An enhanced critical‑incident study of psychotherapy with autistic adults identified both facilitators and barriers to effective therapy, with goal setting and communication emerging as central themes. Facilitators included therapists who offered written materials, used clear, concrete language, checked understanding, and allowed time for reflection between sessions, which supported clients to clarify and communicate therapy goals. Barriers included pressure to respond quickly, over‑reliance on open‑ended questions without scaffolding, and therapists imposing their own priorities (e.g., eye contact, “social skills”) that conflicted with clients’ values.[^2_5]

These findings point toward adapted methods for preference elicitation in psychotherapy: incorporating asynchronous written reflection, explicitly inviting clients to reject or re‑prioritize goals, and framing goal discussions around quality of life and identity‑congruent functioning rather than symptom reduction alone. They also highlight the importance of negotiating goals as an ongoing process rather than a one‑off intake event, given that many autistic adults need repeated exposure and processing time to crystallize priorities.[^2_5][^2_3]

## Social communication and preference/intent expression

Social‑communication research focusing directly on preference expression in autistic adults is relatively sparse but offers a few relevant strands. Smartphone‑based monitoring shows that autistic adults are as socially active as neurotypical peers but preferentially use written communication, which supports self‑expression and reduces social anxiety. This suggests that interventions which rely heavily on synchronous oral discussion may systematically underestimate autistic adults’ ability to articulate nuanced preferences and intentions.[^2_6]

Work on autonomy and self‑determination emphasizes that opportunities to express preferences must include the possibility of saying “no,” and that non‑engagement with offered options may be a meaningful expression of intent rather than a deficit in decision‑making. In practice, this means that preference‑elicitation methods need to interpret refusal and non‑selection as data, and to broaden or adjust options accordingly rather than intensifying pressure to choose. Discourse‑based work on autistic focused interests also suggests that interest‑based exchanges can support richer communication, improved social motivation, and mutual understanding, implying that using focused interests as a starting point may facilitate more authentic discussion of goals and values.[^2_12][^2_1]

## Gaps and directions for systematic evidence

A rapid review on quality of life in autistic adolescents and adults from an OT perspective highlights significant QoL discrepancies and emphasizes the centrality of self‑determination and meaningful participation as targets of intervention. However, it does not systematically appraise tools or protocols for eliciting preferences and priorities, and most included studies treat “goals” and “needs” as outcomes rather than examining the processes by which autistic adults articulate them. Similarly, OT practice guidelines for autistic people across the lifespan foreground self‑determination and person‑centered planning but are narrative rather than systematic in their treatment of assessment and goal‑setting methods.[^2_13][^2_8]

Overall, there is a notable lack of formal systematic reviews specifically on preference elicitation and priority articulation in autistic adults in clinical or occupational contexts. Existing work is dominated by qualitative studies on self‑determination and psychotherapy, participatory design of goal‑setting tools, and practice guidance integrating neurodiversity‑affirming principles. For an evidence synthesis, you would likely need to pull together adjacent literatures: self‑determination and autonomy, executive functioning and decision‑making, participatory goal‑setting tool development, psychotherapy process studies, and OT/social‑work practice guidelines.[^2_8][^2_3][^2_4][^2_5]

# Interventions improving goal specificity and self-articulation in ADHD and

autistic adults. Controlled studies, validated clinical protocols, or
practitioner-documented methods with reported outcomes. Outcomes of interest:
goal clarity, specificity, actionability, or follow-through. Peer-reviewed
or practitioner-published.

Across ADHD and autism, the most relevant interventions for improving goal specificity and self‑articulation are metacognitive/goal‑management programs, structured coaching or telehealth OT, and neurodiversity‑affirming, co‑designed goal‑setting tools. Direct measurement of “goal clarity/specificity” is rare; most studies report proximal outcomes such as quality of goals, satisfaction with self‑selected goals, or follow‑through on personally chosen targets.[^3_1][^3_2][^3_3][^3_4][^3_5]

## Key interventions in adults with ADHD

- **Goal Management Training (GMT) – group interventions**
    - Open‑trial and controlled studies of GMT in adults with ADHD combine psychoeducation about lapses in “autopilot” with structured training in stopping, goal checking, and stepwise planning. Across studies, participants report improved self‑regulation in everyday life and better management of complex tasks, with objective gains in inhibitory control and attentional control; qualitative accounts specifically describe increased awareness of goals, more deliberate goal formulation, and better breaking down of tasks, although goal specificity per se is not quantified.[^3_6][^3_3][^3_7][^3_8][^3_9]
- **Goal‑focused cognitive remediation (psychiatric outpatient setting)**
    - A randomized controlled trial tested a short individual cognitive‑remediation program explicitly organized around formulating patient‑generated goals and practicing cognitive strategies in relation to those goals. Sessions focused on collaboratively generating concrete goals, linking them to everyday difficulties, and using metacognitive strategies to pursue them; participants showed improvements in functioning and psychological well‑being that were maintained at 8‑month follow‑up, and the authors attribute part of the effect to the structured goal‑formulation and review process. The study does not provide a separate metric of “goal quality,” but it operationalizes the intervention as “goal‑focused” and uses repeated refinement of patient goals as a mechanism of change.[^3_2]
- **Work‑MAP: telehealth metacognitive work‑performance intervention**
    - Work‑MAP is an 11‑session, self‑tailored telehealth OT intervention for adults with ADHD that targets work performance on self‑selected goals using metacognitive strategy training, self‑monitoring, and environmental modification. In a randomized controlled trial, adults receiving Work‑MAP showed strong to moderate improvements in performance and satisfaction on their own work goals (measured with Canadian Occupational Performance Measure) and in executive functioning and quality of life at post‑treatment and 3‑month follow‑up. While the primary outcomes are performance and satisfaction, the protocol centers on eliciting personally meaningful work goals and iteratively operationalizing them into actionable tasks, which functionally increases goal clarity and follow‑through.[^3_1]
- **Other ADHD programs with structured goal components**
    - Psychoeducational group programs for newly diagnosed adults with ADHD incorporate patient‑centred modules where participants identify and discuss personal treatment and life goals; pilot RCT data show good feasibility and satisfaction, but do not yet quantify change in goal specificity. Internet‑based CBT protocols for adult ADHD also employ written, homework‑based identification of personally relevant targets (e.g., procrastination, time management), though published protocols focus outcome reporting on symptom severity, not on goal articulation.[^3_10][^3_11][^3_12]


## Goal‑setting and self‑articulation in autistic adults

- **Neurodiversity‑affirming coaching combining SDLMI + SMART + GAS**
    - A neurodiversity‑affirming coaching program paired university students (coaches) with autistic adults to work on three evolving, self‑chosen goals over several weeks, explicitly combining three frameworks: Self‑Determined Learning Model of Instruction (SDLMI) for broad, autonomously chosen goals; SMART criteria for specificity; and Goal Attainment Scaling (GAS) for graded actionability. Participants typically completed about one goal per week, and the combined framework allowed lofty, identity‑oriented overarching goals to be translated into specific, measurable steps while preserving autonomy. Reported outcomes include high acceptability, frequent goal completion, and the ability to flexibly revise or abandon goals that no longer fit, which the authors present as evidence that the method improves both goal clarity and follow‑through.[^3_5]
- **Autistic Adolescent/Adult Goal Setting Tool (AAGST)**
    - The AAGST is a co‑designed, card‑sort tool intended to help autistic adolescents and adults identify, prioritize, and communicate personally meaningful goals across life domains. Evaluation data from the developer report (practitioner‑published) indicate that compared with previous goal‑setting methods, the AAGST helped participants generate a greater number and broader range of goals and improved their ability to rank and discuss priorities, as rated by both participants and practitioners. The tool is not a standardized test, but observational outcomes suggest increased engagement in goal‑setting sessions and more specific statements of what participants wanted to work on.[^3_4][^3_13]
- **Neurodiversity‑affirming, goal‑focused coaching and OT**
    - A neurodiversity‑affirming coaching model for autistic adults describes using SMART goals explicitly framed around “what matters to you,” integrating strengths and preferred communication modes, and using weekly scaling (0–10) to track progress on specific action steps. Practitioners report that combining SMART with GAS and allowing participants to change or abandon goals based on lived experience promotes more authentic, specific goal articulation and reduces goal statements that reflect external pressures. OT practice guidelines for autistic people across the lifespan recommend collaborative, preference‑based goal setting and note clinical reports that this increases client engagement and alignment between goals and everyday activities, though formal outcome metrics on goal clarity are not provided.[^3_14][^3_5]


## Cross‑cutting methods and mechanisms

- **Metacognitive and goal‑management approaches**
    - Across ADHD and autism, the most consistent signal is that metacognitive training plus structured goal‑management (e.g., GMT, Work‑MAP, neurodiversity‑affirming coaching) supports clearer, more actionable personal goals by teaching people to pause, define the goal in concrete terms, break it into steps, and monitor progress. These programs often combine psychoeducation about executive functions with in‑session practice on personally chosen goals, which appears critical for transfer to real‑world follow‑through.[^3_3][^3_2][^3_5][^3_1]
- **Use of structured frameworks (SMART, GAS, SDLMI)**
    - SMART criteria and Goal Attainment Scaling are commonly embedded to enforce specificity (who/what/when/how) and to operationalize varying levels of success, providing both clarity and a graded outcome metric. SDLMI adds a self‑determination layer, ensuring that overarching goals originate from the person’s own values and preferences, with structured reflection questions guiding articulation of “what do I want?” and “what can I do to make this happen?”.[^3_5]
- **Tools that externalize and scaffold goal identification**
    - Card‑sort tools like the AAGST, as well as structured telehealth protocols, externalize the process of identifying and ranking goals via visual prompts, written reflection, and rating scales. These methods reduce working‑memory and language load, making it easier—especially for people with executive and communication differences—to explore options, compare them, and then articulate more specific, prioritized objectives.[^3_4][^3_1][^3_5]
- **Iterative, evolving goals rather than one‑off goal setting**
    - Interventions that treat goals as “evolving”—revisited weekly, open to revision, scaling, or abandonment—report better engagement and more authentic goal articulation than those that lock in goals at baseline. This is particularly salient in autism interventions, where the ability to change course based on sensory, social, or identity considerations is framed as part of self‑determination rather than non‑compliance.[^3_7][^3_5]


## Evidence gaps

Very few studies in either ADHD or autistic adult populations use direct measures of goal clarity or specificity as primary outcomes; most infer improvements from performance on self‑selected goals, self‑regulation ratings, or qualitative feedback on the goal‑setting process. There are also no head‑to‑head comparisons of different goal‑setting frameworks (e.g., GMT vs SMART+GAS) specifically on goal articulation parameters in these groups.[^3_3][^3_1][^3_4][^3_5]

# Alexithymia and difficulty articulating goals, preferences, or intentions

in neurodivergent populations. Prevalence, mechanisms, and implications
for elicitation method design. Peer-reviewed or practitioner-published.
Flag where evidence is contested or thin.

Alexithymia is common in autistic (and probably some ADHD) adults and is strongly implicated in difficulties identifying, prioritizing, and expressing internal states that often underpin goals and preferences, but direct evidence on “goal articulation” is still thin and mostly inferential.[^4_1][^4_2][^4_3][^4_4]

## Prevalence and scope in neurodivergent adults

- A systematic review and meta‑analysis using the Toronto Alexithymia Scale found that autistic adults score significantly higher on alexithymia than non‑autistic controls and that about half of autistic individuals meet cut‑offs for alexithymia versus ~5% of non‑autistic people.[^4_2]
- The authors conclude that co‑occurring autism and alexithymia represent a sizable but not universal subgroup, and argue that alexithymia should be treated as a distinct, clinically relevant dimension within autism.[^4_5][^4_2]
- Narrative and empirical work describes alexithymia as difficulties identifying and describing one’s feelings, an externally oriented thinking style, and reduced imaginal capacities, which together restrict access to internal emotional cues that usually guide value‑based decisions.[^4_6][^4_7][^4_1]

Evidence on prevalence in ADHD is emerging and less cohesive; review work suggests that alexithymia can co‑occur with ADHD and other neurodevelopmental conditions, but robust prevalence estimates are lacking and often confounded by comorbid autism or language disorders. This is an area where evidence is clearly limited and contested in terms of whether alexithymia is separable from broader executive and language problems in ADHD.[^4_8]

## Mechanisms: how alexithymia affects goals and preferences

- Conceptual and empirical work in autism proposes that alexithymia reflects disruption at two key stages of internal processing: directing attention to internal emotional states and appraising/labeling those states, making it harder to know “what I feel” and “what that means” in a given situation.[^4_1][^4_6]
- Because emotional signals and bodily feelings often inform what people care about, this deficit can impair the formation of clear, affectively grounded preferences and intentions, especially in ambiguous or multi‑option decisions.[^4_9][^4_1]
- A systematic review of alexithymia in autism highlights bidirectional interactions: alexithymia may shape emotional interpretation and expression, amplifying social‑communication difficulties; at the same time, long‑standing social confusion and invalidation may reinforce alexithymic tendencies.[^4_3][^4_1]

More broadly, alexithymia has been associated with poorer outcomes across multiple psychiatric treatments, presumably because many therapies rely on the ability to notice, differentiate, and verbalize emotions and motivations. These findings generalize to neurodivergent clients: high alexithymia is likely to make it harder to articulate therapy goals, evaluate progress, or refine priorities, although most studies do not measure goal clarity directly.[^4_4][^4_10]

## Links to social communication and decision‑making in autistic adults

- Longitudinal work in autistic adolescents and adults shows that alexithymia is associated with greater social‑communication difficulties and higher anxiety and depression, but its unique contribution beyond autistic traits varies by outcome and study; some models find alexithymia explains additional variance, others find anxiety and intolerance of uncertainty more central. This literature is therefore mixed and still debated.[^4_11][^4_3]
- Studies of emotion recognition and autistic traits show that when alexithymia is controlled, autistic traits often no longer predict deficits in recognizing facial emotions, suggesting that alexithymia may drive some “social perceptual” difficulties previously attributed to autism itself. If emotional signals from others are harder to interpret, this further complicates collaborative preference‑ and goal‑setting that relies on reading social cues.[^4_12][^4_1]

Work on decision‑making and social choices in autistic adults also points to atypical experiential strategies (e.g., increased switching between options, avoidance of extreme emotional cues) that may reflect, in part, altered emotional learning signals. These patterns, combined with alexithymia, may lead to decisions and stated preferences that are more driven by cognitive rules (e.g., risk avoidance, fairness, predictability) than by clearly felt emotional preferences.[^4_13]

## Implications for elicitation method design

**1. Do not assume easy access to internal states**

- Given the high prevalence of alexithymia in autistic adults and its impact on attention to and labeling of internal feelings, methods that rely on open, emotion‑focused questions (“How do you feel about X?”, “What do you want from therapy?”) risk eliciting vague or over‑learned answers.[^4_2][^4_4][^4_1]
- Systematic and narrative reviews recommend that clinicians recognize alexithymia as a trait that can reduce treatment response unless interventions are adapted, for example by explicitly scaffolding emotion awareness and giving more time and structure for reflection.[^4_14][^4_10][^4_4]

**2. Externalize and scaffold preference/goal construction**

- Because alexithymia shifts attention outward and constrains imaginal processes, tools that externalize the task—visual prompts, written lists, card sorts, structured rating scales—can reduce reliance on spontaneous introspection.[^4_7][^4_15]
- The co‑designed Adolescent and Adult Goal Setting Tool (AAGST) for autistic people is a concrete example: it uses domain‑based cards and structured prompts to help users identify and talk about goals; evaluation reports suggest it helps people generate a wider range of goals and discuss them more specifically, effectively scaffolding self‑articulation even when internal emotional cues are less accessible.[^4_15][^4_16]

**3. Allow time and asynchronous, text‑based reflection**

- Real‑world communication data show autistic adults often favor written over spoken channels, which provide more time and control for organizing and expressing internal states. For individuals with alexithymia, asynchronous written tasks (pre‑session questionnaires, email reflections, journaling prompts) can support slow, cognitively mediated access to preferences and goals.[^4_17]
- Psychotherapy research and critical‑incident studies emphasize that pressuring clients to respond quickly and verbally can impede engagement, especially for those with emotion‑identification difficulties; more structured and paced formats are recommended.[^4_18][^4_4]

**4. Differentiate emotions, values, and practical preferences**

- Because alexithymia specifically targets emotion identification and description, it can be helpful to elicit goals and preferences along several parallel channels: concrete practical preferences (sensory, routine, environment), articulated values (e.g., autonomy, predictability), and any emotional aims people can describe (e.g., feeling less overwhelmed).[^4_9][^4_14][^4_1]
- Sensory‑processing research in autistic adults shows that alexithymia partially mediates the link between sensory differences and anxiety/depression, suggesting that “what feels right” may be encoded more through sensory comfort/discomfort than explicit emotional language. Elicitation tools should therefore include structured queries about sensory and environmental preferences as proxies for underlying affective states.[^4_14][^4_9]

**5. Be cautious in interpreting self‑report emotional scales**

- Reviews of alexithymia assessment highlight that standard self‑report instruments may underestimate or distort emotional difficulties in highly alexithymic individuals, because they require insight into exactly the capacities that are impaired.[^4_7]
- For design and evaluation of elicitation methods, this means relying solely on self‑reported comfort with emotion language may be misleading; triangulation with behavioral indicators (e.g., richness of narratives, specificity of goals over time) or informant data is advisable. Emerging transcript‑based profiling approaches aim to capture alexithymia from language use directly, which could eventually inform more tailored elicitation supports, but current tools remain experimental.[^4_19][^4_7]


## Areas where evidence is contested or thin

- **Causality and boundaries in autism:** There is active debate over whether “autistic social‑emotional difficulties” are largely driven by co‑occurring alexithymia or whether alexithymia is only one contributing factor; some studies find alexithymia explains facial emotion recognition or depression better than autism traits, others find autistic traits remain primary for social‑cognitive outcomes.[^4_20][^4_21][^4_3][^4_1]
- **Extension beyond autism:** While conceptual papers and some empirical work discuss alexithymia in ADHD and other neurodevelopmental conditions, data on prevalence, mechanisms, and specific impacts on decision‑making or goal articulation outside autism are sparse and often confounded by co‑occurring conditions.[^4_8]
- **Direct links to goal specificity and follow‑through:** Most studies address emotional awareness, social communication, or treatment response rather than explicitly measuring goal clarity, preference ranking, or intention articulation as outcomes, so implications for elicitation method design are mostly extrapolated from mechanisms and qualitative reports rather than tested head‑to‑head.[^4_4][^4_15][^4_1][^4_2]

# Interoception deficits and their effect on identifying internal states

relevant to goal formation and preference articulation. Evidence in ADHD
and autistic populations. Relationship to alexithymia as a distinct but
overlapping construct. Peer-reviewed or practitioner-published.

Interoceptive processing differences appear highly relevant to how many autistic (and some ADHD) adults access the internal states that normally inform goal formation and preference articulation, but the evidence base is uneven and autism‑heavy.[^5_1][^5_2][^5_3][^5_4]

## Interoception and internal state identification in autism

A recent systematic review and meta‑analysis of interoception in autism concludes that autism is not reliably associated with reduced objective cardiac interoceptive accuracy in adults, but findings for other interoceptive dimensions and age groups are mixed. Studies report reduced, increased, or typical interoceptive abilities depending on whether they measure accuracy, self‑reported sensibility, or awareness and which organ systems are considered. Heterogeneity in tasks, age, and comorbidities is a major source of inconsistency.[^5_5][^5_6][^5_2]

Despite this, qualitative and questionnaire‑based work indicates that many autistic adults experience “interoceptive confusion” (difficulty interpreting bodily signals unless they are extreme), which is associated with emotional dysregulation and alexithymia. In one adult study, autistic participants showed significantly higher interoceptive confusion, higher alexithymia, and more emotional regulation problems than controls, and emotional clarity, alexithymia, and autism explained 61% of the variance in interoceptive confusion. This pattern suggests that, for a substantial subgroup, bodily cues that normally guide decisions and preferences (e.g., comfort, tension, hunger, anxiety) are noisy or hard to interpret, which can blunt the felt basis for goals and preferences.[^5_3][^5_7]

## Interoception in ADHD

Empirical work on interoception in ADHD is sparse. A study motivated by state‑regulation accounts of ADHD tested interoceptive awareness in adults and found no evidence for altered monitoring of bodily state compared with controls, despite well‑documented self‑regulatory difficulties. The authors interpret this as suggesting that self‑regulation problems in ADHD are unlikely to stem from basic failure to monitor bodily state, although they only examined one aspect of interoception. Beyond this, most links between interoception and ADHD are conceptual or practitioner‑level, emphasizing that misreading internal cues (e.g., emotional arousal, fatigue) may contribute to emotion regulation difficulties, but there is little direct evidence connecting interoceptive deficits to goal formation in ADHD adults. This part of the question rests on very thin data.[^5_8][^5_4]

## Relationship between interoception and alexithymia

Across clinical and non‑clinical samples, alexithymia is increasingly framed as a multidomain failure of interoception. Using novel tasks, one study found that people high in alexithymia showed reduced use of interoceptive cues across respiratory, muscular effort, and taste tasks, independent of autism, depression, or anxiety, supporting the view that alexithymia reflects broad impairments in perceiving and using bodily signals. A commentary and related empirical work argue that, when autism and alexithymia are disentangled, alexithymia—not autism—is associated with impaired interoceptive accuracy, and that interoceptive atypicalities seen in autism are best understood as alexithymia‑specific.[^5_9][^5_10][^5_11][^5_12][^5_1]

In autistic samples, several studies show that reduced interoceptive awareness is linked to higher alexithymia and emotional regulation problems. For example, adults with autism in one study showed reduced interoceptive sensitivity and awareness compared with controls; in this group, interoceptive awareness correlated with empathy and alexithymia, and alexithymia mediated the relationship between interoceptive awareness and empathy. Another study found lower interoception scores and higher alexithymia and emotion regulation problems in autism, with interoception negatively correlated with alexithymia. Together, these findings support a model in which interoceptive dysfunction and alexithymia are distinct but tightly linked: atypical interoception undermines emotional awareness, which manifests clinically as alexithymia and downstream social‑emotional difficulties.[^5_13][^5_14][^5_1][^5_3]

However, large‑scale meta‑analytic work indicates that autism is not uniformly associated with reduced cardiac interoceptive accuracy, and some samples show typical interoception despite elevated alexithymia, suggesting the relationship is probabilistic rather than deterministic. There is ongoing debate about the directionality (interoceptive deficits causing alexithymia vs both arising from broader predictive‑processing differences) and about which interoceptive components matter most (accuracy vs sensibility vs awareness).[^5_6][^5_15][^5_16][^5_2][^5_5]

## Implications for goal formation and preference articulation

Because interoception is a key input to emotional experience and valuation, interoceptive confusion can erode the intuitive “felt sense” that typically helps people decide what matters, distinguish “yes” from “no,” and detect misalignment with chosen goals. In autistic adults with interoceptive challenges and alexithymia, internal cues may be weak, delayed, or ambiguous until they become extreme, which can lead to:[^5_16][^5_9][^5_3]

- Difficulty identifying subtle internal states relevant to goals (e.g., low‑grade anxiety about a work context, emerging interest or fatigue) before they become overwhelming.[^5_7][^5_3]
- Greater reliance on external rules, routines, or others’ expectations when setting goals or preferences, because internal signals are unreliable guides.[^5_9][^5_3]
- Challenges evaluating options based on “how they feel” and forecasting emotional consequences, making complex or value‑laden decisions cognitively heavier and more error‑prone.[^5_14][^5_6]

These mechanisms are not usually measured directly in goal‑setting studies but are consistent with qualitative reports of autistic adults who describe needing strong or very concrete bodily or environmental cues before knowing what they want, and with quantitative links between interoceptive confusion, alexithymia, and emotion regulation.[^5_17][^5_3][^5_7]

## Design implications and evidence strength

Interoception–alexithymia research suggests several design principles for eliciting goals and preferences in autistic (and some other neurodivergent) adults:

- **Do not rely solely on “how does this feel?” prompts.** Many individuals will struggle to access fine‑grained bodily or emotional cues on demand; open affect‑focused questions risk blankness or stock answers.[^5_3][^5_14][^5_9]
- **Use external scaffolds and concrete dimensions.** Tools like domain‑based goal cards, structured rating scales (e.g., comfort, predictability, effort), and sensory‑environment checklists help people compare options without relying entirely on subtle interoceptive signals.[^5_18][^5_19][^5_7]
- **Incorporate explicit interoceptive support where feasible.** Narrative and review papers propose that training interoceptive awareness (e.g., mindfulness, body‑scan, biofeedback) can enhance emotional clarity and may reduce alexithymia, potentially improving the quality of internally grounded goals, though controlled data in autistic adults are still emerging.[^5_15][^5_16][^5_7][^5_3]
- **Separate practical, sensory, and emotional aspects of a goal.** Asking about sensory comfort, energy demands, and cognitive load alongside emotions allows preferences to be articulated via more accessible channels when interoceptive–emotional mapping is weak.[^5_20][^5_18][^5_3]

The strongest evidence here is: (a) autism studies demonstrating associations between interoceptive confusion, alexithymia, and emotional dysfunction, and (b) cross‑diagnostic work showing alexithymia as a multidomain interoceptive failure. Evidence is thin or contested regarding: (a) systematic interoceptive impairment in autism at the level of objective accuracy, (b) interoception in ADHD specifically, and (c) direct experimental links from interoceptive interventions to improved goal clarity or preference articulation in neurodivergent adults.[^5_2][^5_4][^5_5][^5_13][^5_1][^5_14][^5_7][^5_3]

# Prioritization and ranking under executive dysfunction. Cognitive mechanisms

that impair the ability to order competing objectives by importance or
urgency in ADHD and autistic populations. Clinical or cognitive psychology
evidence. Methods tested for supporting priority-ordering when goals are
identified but cannot be ranked. Peer-reviewed or practitioner-published.

Executive dysfunction in ADHD and autism disrupts several mechanisms needed to rank goals (holding multiple options in mind, valuing consequences over time, flexibly comparing options, and inhibiting emotionally salient but low‑priority choices), leading to inconsistent or stalled prioritization. Direct intervention studies on “priority‑ordering” are scarce; most relevant methods come from goal‑management, metacognitive, and neurodiversity‑affirming approaches that scaffold ranking once goals are identified.[^6_1][^6_2][^6_3][^6_4][^6_5][^6_6][^6_7]

## Mechanisms in ADHD: urgency, inconsistency, and effort valuation

Neuroeconomic and decision‑making work suggests that ADHD is associated with sub‑optimal and inconsistent temporal decision‑making: people with higher ADHD symptoms switch more often between immediate and delayed options even when one option is objectively better. This is interpreted as difficulty maintaining a stable value representation over time, which undermines consistent prioritization by importance rather than momentary salience. Review work integrating decision‑making and EF in child/adolescent ADHD frames prioritization problems as arising from: impaired working memory (holding multiple options and their consequences), reduced inhibitory control (suppressing tempting low‑priority options), and altered reward valuation, especially for delayed outcomes.[^6_2][^6_3][^6_8][^6_9]

Effort‑related decision studies indicate that individuals with ADHD tend to “discount” rewards that require more cognitive effort, preferring low‑effort/lower‑payoff options. When translated to goal ranking, higher‑effort but high‑value objectives may be consistently down‑ranked or avoided, even when intellectually recognized as important. Clinically, “decision paralysis” has been described in adults with ADHD: high rates of difficulty making choices, strong association with executive dysfunction, and significant impact on work performance and wellbeing, though this construct is only beginning to be formally studied.[^6_10][^6_11]

Taken together, ADHD‑related prioritization problems seem driven by: unstable valuation across time, strong pull of immediacy and effort avoidance, limited working memory for multi‑option comparisons, and impaired inhibition of emotionally or sensory salient but low‑priority tasks.[^6_3][^6_11][^6_2]

## Mechanisms in autism: flexibility, planning, and value comparison

In autistic adults without intellectual disability, EF profiles often show marked difficulties in planning and organizing, alongside cognitive flexibility deficits, even when basic working memory is relatively preserved. Planning‑flexibility impairments make it hard to construct and update a hierarchy of tasks when circumstances or information change, leading to “flat lists” where tasks lack meaningful ordering by importance or urgency.[^6_12][^6_4][^6_1]

Executive‑function reviews in autism highlight that flexibility deficits (set‑shifting, moving between mental rules) are tied to perseveration and difficulty re‑ordering actions when new information appears. For prioritization, this can manifest as: sticking with the initial ordering despite new deadlines, difficulty comparing heterogeneous goals across domains, and challenges integrating social, sensory, and emotional consequences into a single ranked list. Emotional regulation problems in autism—partly linked to EF—can also bias priorities toward avoiding anxiety or sensory discomfort, even where long‑term goals would suggest different ordering.[^6_4][^6_1]

Qualitative and EF‑focused work in autistic young adults reports high levels of difficulties in planning and organization as the most clinically significant domain, with clear associations to daily‑living and employment outcomes. While these studies rarely isolate “priority ranking” as a separate construct, they describe real‑world patterns where tasks are either left unordered, ordered by interest rather than importance, or repeatedly re‑ordered without action—functional equivalents of prioritization impairment.[^6_12]

## Methods supporting priority‑ordering (when goals are identified)

Direct trials on prioritization per se are limited, but several tested or practitioner‑documented methods scaffold ranking once goals are known.

### Goal Management Training (GMT) in adults with ADHD

GMT teaches a stop–check–plan–do sequence and emphasizes constructing and revisiting a “main goal” and subgoals. In adults with ADHD, open and intervention trials show improved inhibitory control and self‑reported everyday functioning, particularly on tasks requiring managing multiple goals and resisting distractions. Although GMT studies do not separately score “priority clarity,” the protocol explicitly trains people to:[^6_5][^6_13][^6_14][^6_15]

- Pause ongoing activity to retrieve the overarching goal.
- Break that goal into ordered steps and check whether current actions match higher‑level priorities.[^6_13][^6_5]

This effectively externalizes priority‑ordering by repeatedly re‑anchoring to the main goal and its sub‑goal sequence.

### Metacognitive OT interventions (Work‑MAP)

Work‑MAP, a telehealth OT intervention for adults with ADHD, uses metacognitive strategy training around self‑selected work goals and requires participants to choose target tasks each week based on importance and feasibility. The therapist guides ranking by collaboratively evaluating: impact on work performance, deadlines, and personal stress, and then selecting 1–2 focus tasks per cycle. RCT results show significant improvements in performance and satisfaction on self‑selected work goals and EF/quality‑of‑life measures. This suggests that structured, values‑ and deadline‑based discussion of “what comes first this week” can support priority‑ordering, even if not measured as a stand‑alone outcome.[^6_6]

### Neurodiversity‑affirming coaching with SMART + SDLMI + GAS (autism)

For autistic adults, a neurodiversity‑affirming coaching program combined:

- SDLMI (to elicit autonomous, personally meaningful goals),
- SMART (to force specificity and timeline), and
- Goal Attainment Scaling (to define graded levels of success for each goal).[^6_7]

Participants worked on three evolving self‑chosen goals and typically completed one per week; they could also revise or abandon goals that no longer fit. Practically, this framework supports priority‑ordering by:[^6_7]

- Requiring selection of a limited number of current focus goals.
- Using GAS scaling (e.g., −2 to +2) to define and monitor progress on each, which indirectly clarifies which goal is “on deck” now.[^6_7]

The study reports high acceptability, frequent goal completion, and successful adaptation when goals changed, implying that structured, evolving prioritization (choose this week’s focus; scale progress) is workable and valued.[^6_7]

### Structured goal‑setting tools (AAGST)

The AAGST card‑sort tool for autistic adolescents and adults lets users first identify many possible goal areas and then narrow and rank them by perceived importance. Practitioner reports indicate that the tool helps users generate more goals across domains and improves their ability to discuss which goals are “more important right now,” compared with unstructured interviews. While outcome data are primarily descriptive, this is one of the few autism‑specific tools that explicitly includes a ranking step (e.g., sorting cards into “now/soon/later”) and shows enhanced engagement with priority decisions.[^6_16][^6_17]

### General EF‑informed strategies (clinical/practitioner literature)

Across ADHD and autism practitioner literature (rather than formal trials), several patterns are recurrently recommended to support prioritization:

- **Limit set size:** restricting active goals to a small number (e.g., 1–3) at any time reduces working‑memory load and decision paralysis.[^6_18][^6_19]
- **Use external matrices or visual orderings:** priority pyramids, importance–urgency matrices, and visual lists help externalize ranking for brains that do not spontaneously order tasks internally.[^6_20][^6_21][^6_22]
- **Anchor to explicit criteria:** collaboratively define what “importance” and “urgency” mean (e.g., deadlines, impact on wellbeing, alignment with values) and then sort tasks against those criteria, rather than asking for global “what matters most?” judgments.[^6_22][^6_18]

These methods have face‑valid alignment with the EF mechanisms described above (reducing working‑memory load, supporting cognitive flexibility by making criteria explicit, helping inhibition by visually highlighting top priorities), but formal empirical evaluation in ADHD/autistic adult samples is limited.
