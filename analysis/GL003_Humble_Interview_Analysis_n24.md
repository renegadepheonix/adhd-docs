# Humble Interview Analysis — 24 Participants (Feb–Mar 2026)

---

## 1. Key findings

- Most participants cannot say what they need from a product. Only 5–7 of 24 generated concrete requirements unprompted. This confirms the community research finding — the absence of positive suggestions is a population-level pattern, not a format artifact.
- The participants who could articulate requirements had all tried and abandoned many tools. Those who hadn't experimented with tools generally couldn't generate requirements even when prompted.
- Where requirements did emerge, they converged on a narrow core: delivery through channels already open, proactive action rather than reminders, and near-zero friction at capture. All variations of the same demand — a tool must not require attention to manage.
- Participants contradicted each other on time blindness, initiation vs completion, and whether task decomposition helps or hurts. There is no single ADHD productivity profile.
- The interviewer introduced clinical terms and solution concepts in virtually every session. Prompted requirements carry less weight than unprompted ones throughout this document.

---

## 2. Can people say what they need?

### The pattern

About 10 of 24 participants could articulate specific product requirements with reasonable spontaneity. The remaining 14 either needed scaffolding — a prototype to react to, a "magic wand" prompt, an interviewer hypothesis to confirm — or produced nothing.

### Examples

At one end: Nilu generated 11 requirements, 9 unprompted. Trip produced 6, all unprompted. Aditi, Arfah, and Jennifer were similarly generative. At the other end: Robert produced zero requirements — his contribution was a principled argument that neurodiverse people cannot self-report needs. Barbara explicitly said she cannot visualise a helpful tool. Andrew, across two sessions, produced one requirement and zero unprompted. Several participants only generated requirements when shown a working prototype or asked a direct "magic wand" question.

### Did designing for it help?

Round 2 was built to elicit product thinking — solution-space framing, prototype demos, scenario questions. It produced marginally more requirements per person (4.3 vs 3.4). But nearly all the uplift came from prototype exposure — participants reacting to things shown to them. The unprompted rate was actually lower in Round 2 (48%) than Round 1 (54%).

The strong articulators in Round 2 would likely have performed similarly in any format. The weakest produced nothing regardless of what was put in front of them.

### An observation about who could articulate

Self-awareness alone did not predict requirement articulation. Andrew is the most articulate and self-reflective participant across both rounds. He produced zero unprompted requirements. Robert is similarly perceptive, similarly empty-handed.

What the strongest articulators had in common was dense tool-rejection history. Nilu, Trip, Aditi, Arfah, and Jennifer have all tried many tools and abandoned them. Participants who described fewer past tool experiments — Viktoria, Barbara, Manny — could not generate requirements even when directly prompted. We can't say from 24 interviews whether this is causal or coincidental, but it was a consistent pattern.

### "I don't know what I need"

At least 7 participants said some version of this. Andrew theorised it as structural: the ADHD condition means the person most affected is least equipped to specify the remedy, because specification requires the same executive functions the condition impairs. Robert put it more directly — "massive presumption that people know what they need" — and framed it as a fundamental problem with the research method, not personal failing.

Barbara and Viktoria couldn't articulate needs but didn't theorise why. Manny produced one requirement then dismissed it. Sophie and Taran hedged extensively.

### Connection to community research

The forum analysis identified a "total lack of positive suggestions" in ADHD community discussions. The worry was that forums select for complaint. The same pattern holds in 1:1 interviews. Positive, specific, unprompted product suggestions came from roughly 5–7 of 24 engaged, reflective participants in 1:1 settings. The forum finding was not about forums.

## 3. The requirements that did emerge

### Delivery friction / no new apps

Aditi: "I barely want to use my apps that I have right now. I don't want another fifth app." She wanted notifications via WhatsApp, not a new interface. Arfah's primary capture tool is a WhatsApp group she named "Me, Myself and I" — single thread, always open, no login. Nilu described login requirements during a capture window killing the idea she was trying to save. A tool that requires installation and a context-switch is already losing to WhatsApp before first use.

### Proactive, anticipatory action

Trip, unprompted: "I need something that can anticipate what my needs are going to be and surfaces that." And: "Do the hard things that are hard for me. Don't cherry pick the easy stuff off the top." Aditi built her own AI agent (Thomas) for email triage and confirmed she'd want it to act before she asked. Zofia independently described wanting to build a personal AI executive assistant. Matthew wanted human-in-the-loop bulk action: "Tell me what you're about to do and then let me say yes or no." The shared demand: tools that reduce cognitive load rather than add to it.

### Low-stimulation UI

Aditi on rejecting Notion: "I go into that thing and then I get overwhelmed and then I just leave it." Her stated requirement: "I want simple like black and white, grayscale. I don't want any more stimulation." Jennifer added that containers can't feel coercive — "I don't necessarily like being told, right, these are the things that you have to do." Both unprompted, both grounded in tools that failed them.

### Cross-system integration

Larissa: "I'm the one connecting" — she manually synchronises her calendar, Notion, client tools, and messaging. Matthew named cross-platform fragmentation as his biggest unmet need. Sophie, unprompted, described wanting something that sits in WhatsApp and Teams and can be queried for a summary. The shared problem: participants who described the most executive function difficulty were also describing the most manual integration work.

### Voice capture with actionable output

Arfah's workflow — voice dump into Whisper, get transcript, feed to ChatGPT — has survived as a stable tool. The key: input is voice, output is structured text, no AI back-and-forth required. Aditi independently described the same asymmetry: voice in, text out. Nilu added the next step: once captured, the idea needs to be taggable against a project or person for immediate action.

### Context-aware notifications

Arfah: "If I get it, say at 9am and I'm in the midst of like getting my daughter out the door. I'm just not gonna do it." Her requirement: anchor notifications to context events (arriving at desk, 10 minutes before first meeting), not clock times. Zofia wanted a one-click focus mode. Sarah wanted zone-time awareness without having to trigger it manually.

### Self-accountability without guilt

Ash, after seeing an accountability feature showing others' progress: "when you do have those bad days and you see a lot of other people progressing, the first thing was guilt." His requirement: "I would love to use it as just a tool for me to keep myself accountable to me and remove the guilt from me." Kevin wanted emotional difficulty tagging on tasks with a historical log — proof that he'd gotten through hard tasks before.

### Rejections as requirements

The clearest product signal often came from people explaining why something doesn't work.

Andrew on reminders: "The moment you tell me go do that task, you've literally taking it to the back of the queue... you have taken away the dopamine kick." Any notification must be opt-in. Externally-pushed reminders harm motivation for some profiles.

Barbara on her dopamine economy: "If I had managed to get the blueberry muffins done, I would have had a high for maybe the rest of the afternoon... And only I know that." A tool that prioritises rationally without mood input will recommend the wrong task.

Trip on AI doing easy tasks: "It's never right... it's usually a complex problem that I haven't figured out myself." AI assistance is most needed at high complexity, not for templates.

Arfah on fixed-time routines: her 9am ChatGPT prompt fails because mornings are unpredictable. Context events beat clock times.

Ash on social comparison: community progress feeds generate guilt on bad days. Accountability features need asymmetric or aggregated views, not individual streams.

## 4. Where participants agreed and disagreed

### Near-universal agreement

Tool abandonment. Nearly every participant has tried multiple tools and abandoned them. The reasons vary — novelty decay, shame after a lapse, UI complexity, setup overhead — but the pattern is consistent.

Email is broken. Sarah: "too many emails, freeze, shut down." Trip leaves emails unread as a consciousness marker. The failure stacks: prospective memory failure, initiation barrier, decision fatigue, emotional avoidance.

Working memory compensation is constant. Every participant has built some external memory system — notebooks, WhatsApp self-groups, browser tabs, voice memos, sticky notes. The form varies; the drive is identical.

### Genuine disagreements

Time blindness. Andrew: "All the time. All the time." Jennifer: "Everything's five minutes." But Sarah is always two minutes early. Kate explicitly rejected the concept — "no time blindness, hyperaware of time, clock in every room." These are not edge cases.

Initiation vs completion. The dominant framing treats initiation as the primary barrier. Ash directly corrected the interviewer: "Getting started is NOT my issue." Kevin: "Finishing is primary struggle, not starting." Both corrections were participant-initiated pushback — among the most reliable data points in the dataset.

Task decomposition. Kate's primary coping strategy is Time Tetris — chunking everything into 5/15/120-minute blocks. Arfah uses ChatGPT micro-step decomposition. Both find it effective. Trip rejects it — checklists break the big-picture view his visual cognition depends on. Nilu works in sessions, not tasks. Neither side is wrong; they describe different ways of working.

Body doubling. Essential for Taran ("always when there's someone else on the other end"), Viktoria ("human decency pushes me"), and Ash (accountability group was "the healthiest year"). Rejected by Marissa and unwanted by Andrew.

### What the disagreements mean

There is no single ADHD productivity profile. A product designed for the time-blind, initiation-barrier person will be wrong for the time-aware, completion-barrier person. Robert went further: "No such thing as a neurodiverse person." That's probably too strong. But any product claiming to address "ADHD productivity" broadly will miss large portions of its audience.

## 5. Novel signal

We include findings here only where 3 or more participants independently described the same thing without prompting from each other or the interviewer.

### Completion failure as distinct from initiation failure *(4+ participants, unprompted corrections of interviewer)*

Ash and Kevin both corrected the interviewer's assumption that initiation is primary. Aditi described completion tasks as "perceived as low-value." Multiple participants described the last 10–20% of a task as lacking reward, urgency, or novelty — and disproportionately abandoned. Products that track initiation without designing for completion incentives miss the actual drop-off point.

### Strategic day write-off *(3 participants, self-framed as positive)*

Nilu: "if the day is sufficiently low mood... I'd rather write the day off." Kate writes off all of August annually. Both frame this as rational triage, not failure. Tools built on consistent daily engagement are structurally incompatible with this strategy. A streak architecture punishes the write-off. A tool that accommodates planned non-engagement and picks up cleanly afterward is a different design brief.

### Integration tax *(3 participants, all unprompted)*

Larissa, Matthew, and Nilu all described the same problem: they are manually synchronising their own tools despite describing significant executive function difficulty. If the product opportunity is anywhere in this data, integration and orchestration is a strong candidate.

### Boom-bust as stable operating mode *(4+ participants, structured lives around it)*

Trip, Kate, and Ash described recurring cycles of intense overwork followed by mandatory recovery — and have structured their lives around it rather than trying to eliminate it. Self-employment, project-based work, monthly accountability. The question for product is not how to smooth the curve but how to support performance within it.

### Messaging as the real capture competition *(4 participants, all independently arrived)*

Arfah, Aditi, Zofia, and Nilu independently arrived at consumer messaging apps as their primary capture tool. A new capture tool is not competing against Notion or Obsidian. It is competing against WhatsApp — always open, zero login, familiar. For these participants, any tool requiring a context switch starts at a disadvantage against something they open dozens of times a day.

## 6. Method and limitations

24 participants, all diagnosed or self-identified ADHD, predominantly knowledge workers in professional and creative roles. Single interviewer across both rounds.

Round 1 (12 interviews): problem-space, open-ended about work and productivity challenges. Round 2 (12 interviews): solution-space, designed to elicit product thinking, included prototype demos.

**Interviewer leading.** ~152 instances identified across 23 interviews. The interviewer introduced clinical terms (time blindness, activation energy, body doubling, hyperfocus) and proposed tool concepts before participants could generate their own framing. Prompted requirements carry less weight than unprompted ones. The most reliable data points are participant pushbacks — corrections of the interviewer's assumptions.

**Self-selected sample.** These are people who volunteered to discuss their ADHD. They are likely more reflective and tool-aware than the general population. The people least able to articulate needs in this sample still showed up and tried. The portion who wouldn't engage at all is invisible.

**Employment skew.** Notable cluster of self-employed and founder-stage participants. Employment structure shapes both problems and workarounds.

**Unprobed topic.** Hormonal and menstrual cycle variability as an ADHD amplifier was nearly unaddressed despite roughly half the sample being female. Kate mentioned cycle-tracking; Larissa trailed off on female presentation. This is likely an interviewer blind spot, not an absence of experience.
