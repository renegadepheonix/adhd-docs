# P1C: Job Loss Patterns and Economic Impact

> **Scope**: Job loss statistics, employment instability patterns, and economic costs of ADHD in the workplace. Complements P1A (task-level impairments) with macro-level outcomes. Evidence tier: Tier 1.
> **Relationship to P1A**: P1A documents the specific task failures and behavioural impairments. This file documents the downstream consequences — who gets fired, how often, and what it costs.

---

## 1. Termination and Firing Rates

Key statistics from employment research include:
- **60% more likely to be fired** from a job compared to adults without ADHD (Barkley, 2008) [^1][^7]
- **66% increase in risk of being fired** compared to typically developing individuals (Murphy & Barkley, 1996; Kessler et al., 2005) [^4]
- **20% of diagnosed ADHD adults** report having been fired from work (WFIRS data), versus 11% of community controls — though this difference did not reach statistical significance, suggesting many ADHD adults' problems remain below the termination threshold [^4]

---

## 2. Job Instability Patterns

- Adults with ADHD hold significantly **more jobs** since leaving high school, indicating chronic instability [^30]
- Only **22-24% of adults with ADHD in Norwegian studies** were in regular work versus 79% of controls [^9]
- In U.S. samples, only **34% of ADHD adults were employed full-time** versus 59% of controls [^9]
- A national survey found only **half of adults with ADHD could hold a full-time job**, compared to 72% of adults without the disorder [^10]
- Adults with ADHD are **3 times more likely to quit impulsively** — sometimes due to boredom alone [^1][^7]

---

## 3. The Inattention Finding

Research consistently identifies **inattention** as the strongest predictor of occupational impairment — more so than hyperactivity or impulsivity:

- Inattention symptoms showed the strongest correlations (**r = 0.40-0.60**) with work-related problems across both community and clinical samples [^4]
- In a Norwegian study (N=149), **inattention significantly predicted employment status**, while hyperactivity/impulsivity, comorbidities, and demographics did not [^9]
- A mediation analysis confirmed inattention (but not hyperactivity/impulsivity) had a "stronger-than-anticipated role" in occupational outcomes [^9]

A 2024 study identified **self-management to time** as the most significant executive function deficit mediating the relationship between ADHD and job burnout — the strongest mediator in the model [^18].

---

## 4. The Discrepancy Finding

A notable finding is that **ADHD adults often feel their performance is worse than it appears to supervisors**. The most common impairment reported (69%) was **"problems working to your potential"** — an internal sense of underperformance — while formal performance evaluations and firing rates were substantially lower (20-23%) [^4][^36].

This suggests ADHD adults:
- Experience significant distress about self-perceived underperformance
- May maintain employment despite internal struggles
- Often feel they are working harder to compensate for ADHD-related challenges

However, employers who were **blinded to ADHD diagnosis** still rated ADHD employees as having significantly greater inattentiveness, poor punctuality, time management problems, and overall poorer work performance than controls [^9].

---

## 5. Economic and Productivity Impact

The workplace costs of ADHD are substantial:
- Adults with ADHD miss on average **more than three weeks of work productivity annually** (WHO data) [^50][^51]
- The World Health Organization reported that 3.5% of workers worldwide have ADHD, resulting in **143 million days of lost production** annually [^49]
- ADHD is associated with a **4-5% reduction in work performance**, **2.1x risk of sickness absence**, and **2.0x risk of workplace accidents** [^49]
- Excess annual cost per ADHD worker estimated at **$4,336** (excluding accident costs) [^9]
- Workers with ADHD average **8.4 excess sickness absence days per year**, plus 21.7 days of reduced work quantity and 13.6 days of reduced work quality annually (WHO data from 10 countries) [^49][^50]

---

## 6. The Swedish Population Study

A 2021 Swedish population study (N=~64,000; 9,718 with ADHD, 48,590 controls, and 5,582 siblings) found that individuals with ADHD had significantly worse employment outcomes across all major categories (Jangmo et al., 2021) [^3]:

- **10.2x higher risk of disability pension** (adjusted hazard ratio vs. controls)
- **2.7x higher risk of long-term sickness absence**
- **1.7x higher risk of unemployment**

Sibling comparison data from the same study provides a particularly strong signal by controlling for shared genetics and family environment: adults with ADHD averaged **34 sickness absence days annually** compared to **8 days for siblings without ADHD** [^53]. This 4x difference within families suggests the effect is attributable to ADHD itself rather than confounding socioeconomic factors.
