# P2E: User Interview Research

> **Scope**: Primary qualitative research from 12 structured interviews with ADHD adults (Feb 2026). Covers key themes, biggest pains, what works, what doesn't work, tools tried, and needs/opportunities identified from participant accounts.

## Key Themes

- **Understanding beats features.** > "The support for me is understanding. It's understanding and acceptance." — *Andrew*. Tools that demonstrate awareness of how neurodiverse minds work will outperform those that simply add more features.
- **Simplicity is non-negotiable.** Every participant who succeeded did so with lightweight, low-friction approaches. Complex tools create additional cognitive load and are quickly abandoned.
- **Autonomy matters deeply.** Reminders and nudges often backfire. People want to feel in control. The key is proactive support without nagging.
- **No single solution fits all.** What works for Andrei (compression, AI, minimalism) is different from what works for Barbara (handwriting, structure, body doubling), which is different from what works for Trip (delegation, AI prosthetics, Perplexity sandboxes).
- **AI is already transformative for this group.** But current tools have friction points (login barriers, upload limits, shallow context) that limit their usefulness.
- **The productivity paradox.** > "I can be organised or I can be productive. I can't be both." — *Trip*
- **Neurodiversity is an innovation advantage.** > "A lot of neurodivergent ideas are fundamentally innovative in the sense that they're just coming from a different angle." — *Matthew*. > "People are exactly the way they're intended to be. You need that kind of diversity for group survival and for things to move forward." — *Trip*

## Biggest Pains

### Executive function and "initial inertia"

This is the foundational struggle. It is not a refusal to work, but a **physiological inability to bridge the gap between intention and action**.

- **The "synaptic break":** Andrew describes physically lying on the sofa screaming at himself to move, but being unable to. > "There's just a break in the synapses." — *Andrew*
- **The waiting mode:** Andrei describes sitting for eight hours doing "literally anything else" to avoid a single task.
- **The finishing block:** Kevin notes that starting is easy (passion), but completing is the failure point.

### Time blindness and "time optimism"

Participants do not perceive time linearly or accurately. This manifests in two distinct ways: losing track of the present, and miscalculating the future.

- **Time optimism:** Barbara believes a cappuccino takes two minutes (it often takes her eight). She plans her day based on this optimism, leading to inevitable over-scheduling.
- **The "focus mode" trap:** Andrew notes that a task takes 10 minutes if he is hyperfocused, forgetting he might not be in that state when the time comes.

### Hyperfocus disruption and the "cost of interruption"

Focus is a high-cost resource. When attained, it is fragile. Breaking it is not just an annoyance; it triggers an emotional "fight" response.

- **The anger response:** Andrew and Sarah both describe visceral anger when interrupted. Andrew calls it "defiance".
- **Context loss:** When pulled out of focus, the "mental stack" collapses. It takes significant energy to rebuild the context of what they were doing.

### Setup anxiety and "blank slate" paralysis

Configurability is often a burden, not a feature. When a tool opens to a blank page or complex settings, it triggers immediate anxiety about "doing it wrong".

- **Setup fatigue:** Doug expresses frustration with instructions written by experts ("absolutely pathetic").
- **Decision paralysis:** Nilu needs "safe defaults" because making decisions on how to use a tool drains the energy needed to actually use it.

### The "hoard and purge" cycle (information overload)

Neurodiverse minds generate and collect ideas faster than they can organise them.

- **Nested rabbit holes:** Doug describes searching for one thing, finding a link, and getting "nested quite deeply" until the original purpose is lost.
- **Hoarding:** Trip describes his digital life as "hoard and purge". He collects tens of thousands of Notion pages until it becomes unmanageable, then abandons it.

### Masking, burnout and guilt

The emotional toll of trying to function in a neurotypical world.

- **The cost of masking:** Matthew "completely burnt out" from the sheer effort of hiding his traits.
- **Guilt-driven work:** > "I'm driven so much by guilt and fear that I'm going to disappoint you." — *Andrew*

## What Works

### Social accountability (like "body doubling")

The most cited effective strategy. Presence works better than willpower, providing an external anchor that makes starting easier.

- **Parallel play:** Sarah and Barbara thrive on having someone "there", even if silent. It prevents the "drift" into phone scrolling.
- **Commitment anchors:** Manny schedules 9am meetings solely to force himself out of bed.

### Radical simplicity and compression

Systems must have near-zero friction. If a task or idea is too big for working memory, it is discarded or ignored.

- **Working memory limits:** Andrei's rule: > "If I can't keep it in my working memory, it's too big." — *Andrei*
- **The "three thing" list:** Barbara limits her to-do list to three or four items to ensure she gets the "dopamine boost" of crossing them off.

### AI as a "warm" cognitive prosthetic

AI is not just a tool; it is a "judgement-free" companion that bridges the gap between messy thoughts and structured output.

- **The "safe" listener:** Taran and Matthew treat AI as a "friend" or "pet neurotypical". It provides a safe space to test ideas without the social anxiety of boring or annoying a human colleague.
- **The "vomit draft" cleaner:** Taran uses voice-to-text to "brain dump" messy thoughts, then asks AI to "categorise and analyse" them.

### Audio and recording as an "attention buffer"

Recording conversations is a strategy to remain present. It unblocks executive function by removing the need to multi-task (listen + write).

- **The attention split:** Trip records everything so he can > "attend to the conversation and not worry about taking notes" — *Trip*. Note-taking competes with listening for neurodiverse brains; recording liberates that capacity.

### Sensory regulation (music and environment)

External stimuli are used to hack internal states, creating boundaries and pacing that the brain cannot generate on its own.

- **Music as a timer:** Viktória measures tasks in "songs" (e.g. "this dishwashing is a four-song task").
- **Location anchors:** Andrei works in a specific coffee shop. When the laptop battery dies, work is done.

## What Doesn't Work

### Complex tools and data entry fatigue

Tools that require significant manual input or maintenance add cognitive load rather than reducing it, leading to abandonment.

- **Data entry fatigue:** Andrei rejects tools like Asana because "it's asking you to give me everything". The setup feels like a second job.
- **The "graveyard" effect:** Trip notes that Notion becomes a "hoard" where information goes to die because retrieval is too hard.

### Nudges, nagging and reminders

Alerts that interrupt without context are experienced as demands rather than support, often triggering avoidance or irritation.

- **The "defiance" trigger:** Andrew notes that reminders feel like demands, pushing the task to the "back of the queue" out of spite.
- **Noise vs signal:** Doug points out that an alert 30 minutes before a meeting is useless — it just blocks his view of the clock.

### Text-heavy and "blank slate" tools

Interfaces that present too much information or require immediate decision-making overwhelm executive function before work even begins.

- **Unstructured audio/text:** Viktória hates "read aloud" features that read everything (headers, footers) without context.
- **The "how do you want to start?" question:** Doug notes that tools asking for configuration upfront fail. He needs "sensible defaults" because he doesn't know how he wants to work yet.

## Needs and Opportunities

### Smart email triage and summarisation

Email was the single most widely shared pain point, and no participant had found a satisfactory solution. There is a clear gap for a tool that can surface priority, summarise context and reduce the cognitive cost of engaging with an inbox.

> "If I open my email and it told me exactly where I've left off with something. Like a recap of exactly where I am with each individual project. If it told me what was expected of that email and what I needed to know before even diving into it." — *Sarah*

> "I want you to filter out all this junk that I'm not interested in." — *Trip*

Viktória wants clear hierarchy: > "First paragraph which is the most important information, separated, and then all the detail around it." — *Viktória*

### Cross-platform integration

Participants' information and tasks are scattered across multiple tools and channels. Nobody had found a way to bring everything together, and the friction of switching between platforms was a recurring source of lost context and dropped threads.

> "I think the biggest thing would be something that can draw stuff together across different platforms. It's really hard to operate when you've got some stuff in Gmail, some stuff in Obsidian, some stuff in a LinkedIn chat." — *Matthew*

> "Productivity is more about fluidity of information and task management." — *Nilu*

### Low-friction idea capture that actually resurfaces

Capturing ideas is easy; finding them again is hard. Most participants had some form of note-taking but described systems that became graveyards of forgotten thoughts. The opportunity is in capture that requires zero setup and retrieval that works when you only half-remember what you wrote.

> "It would be great if you guys could make one" — *Sarah* (referring to a tool for capturing and retrieving ideas).

> "I wanted to start immediately, capture the context and then I can always save later. Current tools require login before capture, losing the idea in the process." — *Nilu*

Doug wants searchable idea storage so he can search "radar" and find connected notes.

### Human-in-the-loop automation

Participants want tools that take initiative but don't act without permission. The ideal is an assistant that prepares, drafts and suggests, then waits for a yes or no. **Full automation feels risky and disempowering; full manual control is too much effort.**

> "I want you to do important things and I need to be in the loop. What I need you to do is tell me what you're about to do and then let me say yes or no." — *Matthew*

This was echoed by several participants who want AI that works proactively but keeps them in control.

### Visual and audio-first information delivery

Text-heavy interfaces are a barrier. Participants described responding far better to visual formats, video, audio and bite-sized content. There is an opportunity to present the same information in formats that match how neurodiverse people actually process it.

> "Everything is in bubbles and it's short, but you just keep going and going. It's quite a lot when you think about it, but you never notice it's a lot." — *Viktória* (on manga-style formats)

> "I do the best with anything video related. I listen and watch." — *Sarah*

> "If there were visual red, yellow, green emojis next to the task that I need to be doing, but it knows that I don't like this task. At least I know what I'm getting at." — *Kevin*

### Ambient communication of availability

Several participants described the difficulty of communicating their current state to colleagues, whether that is running late, in deep focus or having a low-energy day. **Passive, automatic signals could reduce social friction without requiring active effort.**

> "If I don't ping a thing or use my phone, by the time it gets to 10 o'clock, I should auto send a message to whoever saying, hey, Nilu's probably gonna be like an extra hour today." — *Nilu*

> "I would be happy to click a button and say, I'm in my zone time. I want to silence all notifications." — *Sarah*

### Gamification for sustained engagement

Standard productivity tools rarely hold neurodiverse attention beyond the first few days. **Gamification, even simple reward mechanisms, can sustain engagement where other approaches fail.** The key is making it feel rewarding rather than patronising.

> "If gamification existed I would probably end up sticking to it because I get some rewards in my mind." — *Taran*

Manny noted that Asana's unicorn animation when tasks are completed creates better engagement than Jira's plain interface.

### Sensible defaults and minimal setup

Any tool that requires configuration before it delivers value will lose most of this audience. The opportunity is to ship with **smart defaults that work immediately** and allow customisation later for those who want it.

Doug: tools should have "sensible defaults" that protect privacy and don't require extensive configuration.

The pattern across all interviews is that tools which require setup before they deliver value are abandoned.

### A digital scrum master

Several participants described wanting something that sits between a to-do list and a personal assistant. Not a reminder system, but a guide that prompts reflection, helps with prioritisation and supports the rhythm of a working day without being intrusive.

Matthew describes needing > "almost like a digital scrum master" — *Matthew* that prompts day reviews and guides structured reflection.

> "I need something that does stuff for me. I need something that can anticipate what my needs are going to be." — *Trip*

### Personalisation for spectrum differences

Neurodiversity is not one thing. ADHD with dyslexia is different from ADHD with autism, which is different from dyslexia with dyscalculia. **Any tool in this space needs to accommodate a wide range of profiles rather than targeting a single archetype.**

> "ADHD plus dyslexia, ADHD plus autism, those are different things, but it becomes ADHD. It's great to tackle that core. But that requirement isn't the same as that requirement." — *Andrew*

> "If you've met one autistic person, then you've met one autistic person." — *Doug*

### B2B opportunity for therapists

One participant raised a practical commercial angle: therapists spend significant session time catching up on context, which is expensive for the client and inefficient for the practitioner. AI summarisation could reduce this waste.

> "The first 15 to 20 minutes just goes in waste of her picking my file out again. If there was an agent that stores it digitally, she can have a 100-word summary. It would save me like £15 to £20 every session." — *Taran*
